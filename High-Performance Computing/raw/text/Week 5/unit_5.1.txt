--- page 5 ---
• We want to be able to handle very large and very small numbers:
- Mass of the electron: 9.109 × 10−31 kg
- Mass of the Sun: 1.99 × 1030 kg
• More than 60 orders of magnitude between these numbers
• We need a representation where the decimal point can move (floating point)
Why floating point?

--- page 6 ---
• The floating point representation has a base (β) and a precision (p) 
• A floating point number is represented by d . dddd × βe :
- d . dddd is the significand (mantissa) which has p digits 
- e is the exponent
• For example 9.109 × 10−31 kg

• Floating point is the most widely used method for representing non-integer values
• IEEE 754 is the most widely adopted standard for floating point arithmetic
• The standard specifies number representations (e.g. single precision, double 
precision)
• The standard also specifies how addition, subtraction, multiplication, division and 
square root operations should behave
IEEE 754  
12

--- page 8 ---
• Support for IEEE 754 was added to C in the C99 standard
• The two most commonly used precisions are single precision and double precision: 
- Single precision: 32-bit (float)
- Double precision: 64-bit (double)
IEEE 754 support in C

--- page 9 ---
• Floating point numbers are an approximation to the real numbers
• Floating point numbers have a finite accuracy and a finite range
• We need to consider the range and accuracy required for a given application
• Double precision floating point arithmetic is the standard in scientific and engineering 
calculations
• Other applications may not require as much precision (e.g. machine learning, 
graphics)
Why double precision?

• There are 52 bits for the mantissa, 11 bits for exponent and 1 bit for the sign (±)
• If the exponent bits are neither all zeros or all ones then the number is said to be a 
“normalised” floating point number
• A normalised floating point number x is represented by:

• In week 3 we saw that finite difference approximations have a truncation error
• Other numerical methods (e.g. calculating a series solution) will have associated 
numerical errors
• There is also a round-off error from the floating point representation of the numbers
• If the error from the numerical method is smaller than the error from the floating point 
representation then the solution is said to be calculated to “machine precision”

• Floating point is used to represent non-integer values
• IEEE 754 is the most common standard for floating point arithmetic
• IEEE 754 double precision uses 64 bits (8 bytes) to represent the number
• Most scientific calculations use double precision arithmetic (double in the C 
programming language) 
• The range of normalised double precision numbers is 10−308 to 10308
• The finite precision of floating point numbers introduces a round-off error into 
calculations
