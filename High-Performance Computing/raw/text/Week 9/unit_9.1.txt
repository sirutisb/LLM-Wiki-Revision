Finite volume methods
7
• Finite differences are easy to 
implement but are not suited to 
complex meshes
• The finite volume method 
considers fluxes (e.g. the flow of 
mass, momentum and energy) 
between adjacent cells
• An example is the OpenFOAM 
open source, finite volume CFD 
software
https://www.cfdsupport.com/OpenFOAM-Training-by-CFD-Support/node140.html 
https://blog.pointwise.com/2021/03/05/this-week-in-cfd-423/

Finite element method
8
• The finite element method 
approximates the solution using 
expansion functions which are 
non-zero over a small region of the 
domain
• Two examples are Nektar++ and 
Firedrake
• Both finite element and finite 
volume methods require solving 
systems of linear equations to 
advance the solution to the next 
time step 
•
http://emps.exeter.ac.uk/engineering/research/ourresearch/computational-engineering/

Elliptic equations
9
• Some PDEs do not contain a time 
dependence so can’t be solved 
with time stepping
• One example is the Poisson 
equation used to calculate self- 
gravity:
∇2φ(r) = 4πGρ(r) 
where φ(r) is the gravitational 
potential, ρ(r) is the density 
distribution and G is a constant 
http://www.astro.ex.ac.uk/people/mbate/

Unit summary
10
• Solving differential equations with a computer requires approximating calculus with 
algebra
• To do this many scientific and engineering applications use numerical methods which 
require solving systems of linear equations
• Finite volume and finite element methods are two examples of methods used in 
computational fluid dynamics which require calculating solutions to linear systems
• Solving other types of equations (e.g. a Poisson equation) can also require solving 
linear systems
