## Question 1 (40 marks)

### 1(a) You are given the task of helping a researcher speed up their program and you suggest parallelising the program. The researcher says: "Parallel programming is too difficult. I've heard that Moores Law is still going. Why can't I get a much faster processor to make my serial program run quicker?" How would you explain to them that they have mis-understood Moores Law and why they can't get a faster single processor core? *(8 marks)*

**Answer:**

The researcher confuses **Moore's Law** with **clock-frequency scaling**:

- **Moore's Law** (transistor density doubling) is still progressing, but it now buys *more cores per chip*, not faster cores.
- **Dennard scaling** — the rule that smaller transistors run faster *and* use less power per unit area — broke down in the mid-2000s. Leakage current and the resulting power-density wall mean further frequency increases produce more heat than air-cooling can dissipate.
- Clock frequencies have been stuck around 2–4 GHz since ~2005. Single-core gains now come only from architectural improvements (wider vector units, better branch prediction, more cache).
- The only way to convert Moore's Law's transistor budget into program performance is to use the *additional cores*. A serial program leaves all but one core idle.

So Moore's Law is not a substitute for parallelism — it is the *reason* parallelism is now mandatory.

*Concepts: [Moore's Law and Dennard Scaling](../concepts/Moores_Law_and_Dennard_Scaling.md), [HPC Programming Languages](../concepts/HPC_Programming_Languages.md)*

### 1(b) The Top500 list ranks the fastest supercomputers in the world according to the performance on the LINPACK benchmark.

#### (i) What is the LINPACK benchmark? *(2 marks)*

**Answer:**

LINPACK / **High-Performance Linpack (HPL)** is a benchmark that solves a dense system of linear equations $Ax = b$ using LU factorisation, measuring sustained floating-point performance in flop/s. The Top500 list ranks systems by their measured HPL performance ($R_{max}$).

*Concepts: [Performance Metrics and Top500](../concepts/Performance_Metrics_and_Top500.md)*

---

#### (ii) Explain the reasons behind the common observation that real-world applications often exhibit lower performance levels compared to the LINPACK benchmark. *(6 marks)*

**Answer:**

LINPACK is engineered to maximise the fraction of $R_{peak}$ achieved. Real applications typically fall well below it because:

1. **Arithmetic intensity** — LINPACK uses dense matrix-matrix operations (BLAS Level 3, DGEMM) with $O(N)$ FLOPs per byte moved; it is heavily compute-bound. Most real codes (sparse solvers, finite-difference stencils, vector ops) have $O(1)$ arithmetic intensity and are **memory-bandwidth-bound**, never approaching $R_{peak}$.

2. **Vectorisation and FMA** — LINPACK's inner loops use the widest available vector units (AVX-512) with fused multiply-add. Many real codes don't, especially legacy and unoptimised software.

3. **Communication overhead** — LINPACK has been heavily tuned to overlap MPI communication with computation. Real codes that perform halo exchanges, reductions, or all-to-all collectives every iteration spend a non-trivial share of their time waiting on the network.

4. **Load imbalance** — LINPACK partitions a uniform workload across processes. Real applications often have heterogeneous work (e.g. adaptive mesh refinement) that creates idle time.

5. **Cache behaviour** — LINPACK is blocked to fit operands into L1/L2 cache. Codes that don't apply cache blocking spend much more time waiting on DRAM.

6. **I/O and other overheads** — Real applications read input, checkpoint, log progress, and synchronise — none of which LINPACK does.

The HPCG benchmark was introduced precisely because it is more representative: it solves a sparse problem and shows performance figures 1–2 orders of magnitude lower than HPL on the same hardware.

*Concepts: [Arithmetic Intensity and Roofline Model](../concepts/Arithmetic_Intensity_and_Roofline_Model.md), [BLAS and Dense Matrices](../concepts/BLAS_and_Dense_Matrices.md), [Memory Hierarchy and Cache](../concepts/Memory_Hierarchy_and_Cache.md), [Performance Metrics and Top500](../concepts/Performance_Metrics_and_Top500.md)*

#### (ii) What is the theoretical peak performance of a single processor core? *(4 marks)*

**Answer:**

$$R_{peak,\,core} = \frac{R_{peak}}{N_{cores}} = \frac{10\,066.33 \times 10^{12}\ \text{flop/s}}{786\,432}$$
$$= 1.2799 \times 10^{10}\ \text{flop/s} \approx 12.8\ \text{GFlop/s}$$

Cross-check: at a 1.6 GHz clock, this implies $12.8 / 1.6 = 8$ floating-point operations per cycle per core (consistent with the Power BQC's QPX 4-wide double-precision SIMD with FMA: $4 \times 2 = 8$).

### 1(d) Matrix operations are often used in high performance computing to solve systems of linear equations.

#### (i) What is the difference between a sparse matrix and a dense matrix? *(2 marks)*

**Answer:**

- **Dense matrix:** most or all elements non-zero, stored as a contiguous 2-D array.
- **Sparse matrix:** the vast majority of entries are zero; specialised formats (CSR, COO, …) avoid storing the zeros and skip the corresponding multiplications.

*Concepts: [Sparse Matrices and CSR](../concepts/Sparse_Matrices_and_CSR.md)*

---

#### (ii) Describe the compressed sparse row format for storing sparse matrices. *(4 marks)*

**Answer:**

Three 1-D arrays for a matrix with `nrow` rows and `nnz` non-zero entries:
1. **`vals[nnz]`** — the non-zero values, in row-major order.
2. **`col_ind[nnz]`** — the column index of each value.
3. **`row_ptr[nrow + 1]`** — `row_ptr[r]` is the index where row `r` starts in `vals`/`col_ind`. Final entry is `nnz + 1` (1-indexed) as a sentinel.

Storage: $2 \cdot nnz + nrow + 1$ entries instead of $nrow \cdot ncol$. Trade-off is **indirect addressing** through `col_ind`, slower than direct dense access.

*Concepts: [Sparse Matrices and CSR](../concepts/Sparse_Matrices_and_CSR.md)*

---

#### (iii) How does the number of bytes of data transferred from memory scale with N for dense matrix multiplication and why? *(2 marks)*

**Answer:**

$O(N^2)$. Each $N \times N$ matrix has $N^2$ entries, and matmul reads $A$, reads $B$, writes $C$ — all $O(N^2)$ in memory traffic. FLOPs scale as $O(N^3)$, so arithmetic intensity grows as $O(N)$.

*Concepts: [BLAS and Dense Matrices](../concepts/BLAS_and_Dense_Matrices.md), [Arithmetic Intensity and Roofline Model](../concepts/Arithmetic_Intensity_and_Roofline_Model.md)*

### 1(e) MPI is a widely used parallel programming technology which can be used to write parallel programs to run on a wide range of architectures. OpenMP is another parallel programming technology which can be used instead of, or in combination with, MPI. Describe four situations where you would choose to use OpenMP instead of, or in combination with, MPI. *(8 marks)*

**Answer:**

1. **Single shared-memory node target.** When the target hardware is one server / workstation with shared memory, OpenMP delivers parallelism without the marshalling overhead of MPI. A 12-core server with one OpenMP program is simpler than 12 MPI ranks on it.

2. **Incremental parallelisation of an existing serial code.** OpenMP only requires `#pragma omp parallel for` and scoping clauses; the serial control flow stays intact. MPI requires re-architecting the code to handle data distribution, halo exchange, and collective communication.

3. **Hybrid MPI+OpenMP for very large clusters.** Use MPI between nodes (one rank per node or per socket) and OpenMP within each node. This reduces the total number of MPI ranks, cuts down on duplicated halo memory, and reduces the number of network messages.

4. **Fine-grained parallelism within a region that pure MPI cannot exploit.** OpenMP `tasks` and dynamic loop scheduling can balance irregular work cheaply. Doing the same dynamic balancing through MPI requires implementing a manager-worker pattern.

5. **Memory pressure.** If each MPI rank's halo / duplicated state is too large to fit `RAM / cores_per_node` per rank, switch to OpenMP threads which share a single copy of the static data.

6. **Quick performance win on a multicore machine.** When the goal is "make this run in 1 hour instead of 8" and the user has one machine, OpenMP is the lowest-effort option.

*Concepts: [OpenMP vs. MPI](../comparisons/OpenMP_vs_MPI.md), [Hybrid Parallelism (MPI + OpenMP)](../concepts/Hybrid_Parallelism_MPI_OpenMP.md), [OpenMP Tasks](../concepts/OpenMP_Tasks.md)*

## Question 2 (30 marks)

### 2(a) The following C program makes a call to the MPI library but the call has been removed and replaced with the "Missing line" comment.

```c
#include <stdio.h>
#include <mpi.h>

int main(int argc, char *argv[]){
    int a[3], b[6];
    int myRank, i;

    MPI_Init(&argc, &argv);
    MPI_Comm_rank(MPI_COMM_WORLD, &myRank);

    for (i=0; i<3; i++){
        a[i] = i + myRank*3;
    }

    for (i=0; i<6; i++){
        b[i] = 0;
    }

    printf("Rank: %d a before: %d %d %d\n",
           myRank, a[0], a[1], a[2]);
    printf("Rank: %d b before: %d %d %d %d %d %d\n",
           myRank, b[0], b[1], b[2], b[3], b[4], b[5]);

    /************* Missing line *************/

    printf("Rank: %d a after: %d %d %d\n",
           myRank, a[0], a[1], a[2]);
    printf("Rank: %d b after: %d %d %d %d %d %d\n",
           myRank, b[0], b[1], b[2], b[3], b[4], b[5]);

    MPI_Finalize();
    return 0;
}
```

When the program is run using 2 MPI processes it produces the following output:

```
Rank: 0 a before: 0 1 2
Rank: 0 b before: 0 0 0 0 0 0
Rank: 1 a before: 3 4 5
Rank: 1 b before: 0 0 0 0 0 0
Rank: 1 a after:  3 4 5
Rank: 1 b after:  0 0 0 0 0 0
Rank: 0 a after:  0 1 2
Rank: 0 b after:  0 1 2 3 4 5
```

Write out the missing MPI call making sure to include all the arguments. *(10 marks)*

**Answer:**

**Reasoning:**
- On rank 0, `b` has changed from `[0,0,0,0,0,0]` to `[0,1,2,3,4,5]`.
- That is precisely the concatenation: rank 0's `a = [0,1,2]` placed at the start, followed by rank 1's `a = [3,4,5]`.
- On rank 1, `b` is unchanged.

This pattern matches **`MPI_Gather`** from `a` into `b`, with rank 0 as the root. Each process contributes 3 ints; the root receives `nprocs × 3 = 6` ints, in rank order, into `b`.

```c
MPI_Gather(a, 3, MPI_INT, b, 3, MPI_INT, 0, MPI_COMM_WORLD);
```

**Argument-by-argument:**

| Arg         | Value            | Meaning                              |
| :---------- | :--------------- | :----------------------------------- |
| `sendbuf`   | `a`              | local data each rank sends           |
| `sendcount` | `3`              | 3 ints per rank                      |
| `sendtype`  | `MPI_INT`        | element type                         |
| `recvbuf`   | `b`              | only used on root; ignored elsewhere |
| `recvcount` | `3`              | size received from *each* rank       |
| `recvtype`  | `MPI_INT`        | type received                        |
| `root`      | `0`              | rank 0 collects                      |
| `comm`      | `MPI_COMM_WORLD` | communicator                         |

It is *not* `MPI_Allgather` — only rank 0's `b` was modified. It is *not* `MPI_Scatter` — the data flows inward from all ranks to root.

*Concepts: [MPI Collective Communication](../concepts/MPI_Collective_Communication.md)*

### 2(b) You are providing programming support to a researcher who is using a C program to perform calculations. They are running the program overnight on a laptop computer and it takes 8 hours to complete. They need to get results from their program more quickly, ideally within an hour. When you look at their program you see that it is a serial program with no parallelisation, and most of the work is in for loops. You know that there is a server with a single 12-core processor which could be used to run the program. You decide to parallelise the program so that it can run on the server.

#### (i) What is the minimum parallel efficiency required on this server to reduce the 8 hour run time to 1 hour? Show your working. *(4 marks)*

**Answer:**

Required speed-up: $S = T_0 / T_N = 8 / 1 = 8$ on $N = 12$ cores.

Parallel efficiency:
$$E = \frac{S}{N} = \frac{8}{12} = 0.667 = 66.7\%$$

So the implementation must achieve **at least 66.7% efficiency** on 12 cores.

*Concepts: [Parallel Scaling](../concepts/Parallel_Scaling.md)*

---

#### (ii) You need to deliver a working parallel program as quickly as possible. Would you choose OpenMP or MPI for parallelising the program? Explain your choice. *(6 marks)*

**Answer:**

**Choose OpenMP.** Justification:

- The target hardware is a **single 12-core node** with shared memory. OpenMP runs there natively; there's no point in paying MPI's distribution overhead for a problem that fits on one node.
- The existing code is **serial with most work in `for` loops** — exactly OpenMP's sweet spot. Adding `#pragma omp parallel for` (with appropriate `reduction`/`private` clauses) typically yields a parallel version in hours.
- The deadline is "as fast as possible". MPI would require restructuring the program to explicitly distribute arrays, code halo or gather/scatter steps, and debug deadlocks — days to weeks of work for no extra speed-up over OpenMP at this scale.
- 66.7% efficiency on 12 cores is achievable with OpenMP for a loop-dominated code, especially if the loops have no carried dependencies.

*Concepts: [OpenMP vs. MPI](../comparisons/OpenMP_vs_MPI.md), [OpenMP](../concepts/OpenMP.md), [Parallel Loops in OpenMP](../concepts/Parallel_Loops_OpenMP.md)*

\begin{pmatrix}

vals    = [ 5,  2,  1,  2,  7 ]

## Question 3 (30 marks)

### 3(a) Consider a two-dimensional partial differential equation (PDE) solver which solves a PDE with one unknown variable u. To solve the PDE it is necessary to calculate values for the second derivative:

$$\nabla^2u = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}$$

This expression is evaluated using the finite difference stencil:

```
del2u = (u(i+1,j) - 2*u(i,j) + u(i-1,j))/(dx*dx) +
        (u(i,j+1) - 2*u(i,j) + u(i,j-1))/(dy*dy)
```

The computation domain for the whole calculation comprises 8192 × 8192 grid points. In addition to these grid points each sub-domain stores halo values which are copies of the data on neighbouring processors.

#### (i) The calculation is domain decomposed in a 16 × 16 configuration such that there are 256 square sub-domains of equal size. How many values need to be exchanged when one halo is sent to one neighbouring process? *(2 marks)*

**Answer:**

Sub-domain size: $\frac{8192}{16} \times \frac{8192}{16} = 512 \times 512$. One halo edge to one neighbour:

$$\text{Values per halo} = 512$$

*Concepts: [Domain Decomposition](../concepts/Domain_Decomposition.md)*

---

#### (ii) How many bytes need to be sent if the values are represented using IEEE754 double precision? *(2 marks)*

**Answer:**

$$512 \times 8 = 4096\ \text{bytes}$$

---

#### (iii) What is the transmission time for sending a single halo over a network with a latency of 1µs (10⁻⁶s) and a bandwidth of 15 GB/s (15 gigabytes per second)? Show your working. *(3 marks)*

**Answer:**

$$t = L + \frac{M}{B} = 1.0 \times 10^{-6} + \frac{4096}{15 \times 10^{9}}$$
$$= 1.0 \times 10^{-6} + 2.731 \times 10^{-7}$$
$$\boxed{t \approx 1.27 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{s} = 1.27\ \mu\text{s}}$$

*Concepts: [Interconnects and Network Topologies](../concepts/Interconnects_and_Network_Topologies.md)*

---

#### (iv) The domain decomposition is modified so that the domain is decomposed in a 32 × 32 configuration such that there are 1024 square sub-domains of equal size. What is the transmission time for one halo with the modified domain decomposition? *(3 marks)*

**Answer:**

Sub-domain: $\frac{8192}{32} \times \frac{8192}{32} = 256 \times 256$. Halo length 256, bytes $256 \times 8 = 2048$.

$$t = 1.0 \times 10^{-6} + \frac{2048}{15 \times 10^{9}} = 1.0 \times 10^{-6} + 1.365 \times 10^{-7}$$
$$\boxed{t \approx 1.14 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{s} = 1.14\ \mu\text{s}}$$

The reduction from 1.27 µs to 1.14 µs is small because the latency term is now dominant — message size has halved but the floor stays at 1 µs.

*Concepts: [Interconnects and Network Topologies](../concepts/Interconnects_and_Network_Topologies.md), [Domain Decomposition Overheads](../concepts/Domain_Decomposition_Overheads.md)*

### 3(b) The "Memory Intensive Service" is an HPC facility hosted by the University of Durham at the Institute for Computational Cosmology (ICC). The system comprises 452 compute nodes, each with 512 GB of RAM, and offering a total of 12 656 cores. All the compute nodes have the same number of processor cores.

Cores per node: $12656 / 452 = 28$.

#### (i) What is the largest number of processor cores available to an OpenMP program running on this system and why? *(3 marks)*

**Answer:**

OpenMP is a **shared-memory** programming model; threads share the address space of one process. On a cluster, that process is confined to a single compute node, so OpenMP cannot reach beyond one node's cores.

**Maximum: 28 cores** — the cores in one node.

*Concepts: [OpenMP](../concepts/OpenMP.md), [OpenMP vs. MPI](../comparisons/OpenMP_vs_MPI.md)*

---

#### (ii) What is the largest number of processor cores available to an MPI program running on this system and why? *(3 marks)*

**Answer:**

MPI uses **distributed memory** with explicit message passing, so it scales across nodes via the interconnect.

**Maximum: 12 656 cores** — every core on the system can host an MPI rank.

*Concepts: [Message Passing Interface (MPI)](../concepts/Message_Passing_Interface_MPI.md)*

---

#### (iii) Unified Parallel C can be used to write parallel programs as an alternative to OpenMP and MPI. According to Wikipedia, UPC is an extension of the C programming language designed for high-performance computing on large-scale parallel machines, including those with a common global address space (SMP and NUMA) and those with distributed memory (e.g. clusters). The programmer is presented with a single shared, partitioned address space, where variables may be directly read and written by any processor, but each variable is physically associated with a single processor. UPC uses a single program, multiple data (SPMD) model of computation in which the amount of parallelism is fixed at program startup time, typically with a single thread of execution per processor. Based on this information what is the largest number of processor cores available to a Unified Parallel C program running on this system and why? *(4 marks)*

**Answer:**

UPC presents a **single shared, partitioned address space** across both shared-memory *and* distributed-memory (cluster) systems. The Wikipedia description explicitly says it works on clusters via PGAS, with one thread per processor.

So UPC is not confined to one node; it can use the whole machine like MPI.

**Maximum: 12 656 cores.**

*(The key point is "partitioned global address space". Each variable is owned by one processor, but any processor can read/write it; the runtime handles off-node accesses by communication. There is no shared-memory hardware requirement.)*

*Concepts: [Cluster Architecture](../concepts/Cluster_Architecture.md), [HPC Programming Languages](../concepts/HPC_Programming_Languages.md)*

### 3(c) Performing large scale calculations requires the use of parallel programs which can scale effectively to use many processors. Understanding the efficiency and scaling properties of a parallel program is important in order to make effective use of HPC systems.

#### (i) Write down an expression for Amdahl's law and say which type of scaling (strong or weak) it describes. You should clearly define the symbols used in the expression. *(3 marks)*

**Answer:**

$$S_N = \frac{1}{s + \dfrac{p}{N}}$$

where:
- $S_N$ is the speed-up on $N$ processors,
- $s$ is the *serial* fraction of the program (the part that cannot be parallelised),
- $p$ is the *parallel* fraction, with $s + p = 1$,
- $N$ is the number of processors.

Amdahl's Law describes **strong scaling** — the total problem size is held fixed, and we ask how runtime decreases as more processors are added.

*Concepts: [Parallel Scaling](../concepts/Parallel_Scaling.md)*

---

#### (ii) According to Amdahl's law, what is the maximum possible speedup of a parallel program with a parallel fraction p? *(2 marks)*

**Answer:**

Take $N \to \infty$:
$$\lim_{N \to \infty} S_N = \frac{1}{s} = \frac{1}{1 - p}$$

The serial fraction is the absolute ceiling — even with infinite processors, the parallel time can never beat $s \cdot T_0$.

---

#### (iii) Write down an expression for Gustafson's law and say which type of scaling (strong or weak) it describes. You should clearly define the symbols used in the expression. *(3 marks)*

**Answer:**

$$S_N = s + p \cdot N$$

where $s$ and $p$ are the serial and parallel fractions of the **parallel runtime** (such that $s + p = 1$), and $N$ is the number of processors.

Gustafson's Law describes **weak scaling** — the problem size *grows* with $N$ so that the work per processor stays constant. Under this regime, speed-up grows linearly with $N$ (no Amdahl ceiling).

*Concepts: [Parallel Scaling](../concepts/Parallel_Scaling.md)*

---

#### (iv) How does run time vary with number of processors for an application which exhibits ideal weak scaling? *(2 marks)*

**Answer:**

Run time is **constant** (independent of $N$). The principle of weak scaling is to grow the problem in proportion to the number of processors, so each processor still has the same amount of work — and therefore the same wall-clock time — regardless of how many are working in parallel.
