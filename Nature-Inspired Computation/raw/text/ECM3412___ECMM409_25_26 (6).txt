Neuromorphic computing
Term originated in 1980s referring to
mixed analogue-digital
implementations of brain inspired
techinques
Now includes a broad range of brain
inspired techniques
von Neumann architecture
Neuromorphic architecture
CPUs for processing & memory for
storing data
Synapses used for storing data & pro-
cessing
Programs used for instructing the
computer
Instructions determined by the struc-
ture of the synapses
Data encoded in binary
Receive spikes as inputs – timing and
magnitude used to encode information

Characteristics and advantages
Parallel operation
All neurons can operate simultaneously
Simpler operations possible compared to parallelised von Neumann
machines
Collocated processing and memory
Faster and lower energy consumption than retrieving from memory
Scalability
Simply add more chips with more neurons
Event driven
Processing occurs when a spike arrives

Neuromorphic computers
SpiNNaker
The SpiNNaker chip (18 ARM cores sharing 128Mbyte memory)
16,000 neurons with 8 million synapses running in real-time with an
energy budget of 1W
One of the neurons is a monitor processor running the OS
The others are fascicle processors modelling a group of up to 1000
neurons
Less for accuracy – focussed on exploring the potential of spiking NNs
2019: SpiNNaker 2 announced – 10× bigger than SpiNNaker
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpiNNaker#/media/File:Spinn_1m_pano.jpg

Neuromorphic computers
IBM TrueNorth
Comprises chips containing 1 million neurons and 256 million synapses
Each neuron has an axon which is used to communicate with other
neurons (or itself)
Chip configured with the Corelet lanaguage – bespoke
object-oriented language
IBM NorthPole
Successor IBM NorthPole was unveiled in 2023
Increased focus on usability for modern AI pipelines
Optimised for real-time processing (e.g. autonomous systems
requiring low latency)
Additional optimisations to increase energy efficiency without
sacrificing accuracy

Challenges
“Traditional” ML approaches are still superior
Lack of cases in which neuromorphic computing outperforms deep
learning approaches
They do outperform hardware alternatives (such as edge GPUs) in
terms of energy efficiency
Possibly because of the reliance on backpropagation?
Usability and access
Lack of accessible and usable hardware and software to support
neuromorphic computing
Some simulators – though often built for a specific purpose – and
larger machines (e.g. SpiNNaker) developed for specific communities
Large training time restricts real-world applications

Challenges with neuromorphic computing
Lack of benchmarks
The presence of benchmarks and challenge problems has driven the
development of deep learning – e.g., ImageNet competition
Some problems are proposed but do not incorporate the temporal
aspect that characterises neuromorphic computing
A suite of challenge problems (ML and beyond) are needed
Defining programming abstractions
There is a need for the developer to design SNNs at the
neuron/synapse level – including parametrisation and connections
This is error prone
Existing examples therefore (e.g.) focus on graph modelling – readily
maps to SNN architecture

An example: neuromorphic cameras
Neuromorphic vision sensors – event-based cameras.
Based on the way in which the eye operates.
Emit spikes based on changes in pixel intensity over time.
Offers significantly increased temporal resolution and greatly reduced
power consumption compared to conventional cameras.
Can be combined with output from conventional cameras using
sensor fusion
Conventional cameras still advantageous in some circumstances.

The future?
