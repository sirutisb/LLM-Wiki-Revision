Spiking Neural Networks
Mimic the brain’s neural activity by transmitting information as
discrete spikes over time – useful for sequential/time-varying data
Much more “biologically plausible” than traditional ANNs
Process data sparsely – neurons only activated when necessary
enabling low-power computing
Applications: Robotics, speech recognition, sensory processing, and
other domains requiring dynamic, temporal pattern recognition.
Information is encoded in spike rates (rate coding), timing (temporal
coding), or distributed across neuron populations (population coding)

Neuron charge
Neuron charge accumulates over time either from input signals or
from signals received from other neurons
When the neuron’s charge reaches a threshold the neuron fires and
emits a signal along each of its outgoing connections
Neurons have a notion of leakage such that below-threshold charge
dissipates over time

Spike-timing-dependent-plasticity
Synapses can adapt their strength
based on activity patterns
The pre-synaptic neuron
transmits a signal that is received
by the post-synaptic neuron
Potentiation: if the pre-synaptic
neuron fires before the
post-synaptic neuron the
connection is strengthened
Depression: if the post-synaptic
neuron fires before the
pre-synaptic neuron the
connection is weakened
Supports unsupervised learning

Optimising SNNs with EAs
Optimise network structure and parameters such as weights, delays,
thresholds. . .
Neural architecture search
Often formulated as a bi-level optimisation problem (e.g. optimise
the structure and optimise the weights)
Periodically switch between optimising the structure (the upper
problem) and the weights (the lower problem)
Challenges
1 Cost of optimisation – more expensive to optimise SNNs than ANNs
and we’re combining with a highly iterative process (the EA)
2 Consider wider co-evolution (e.g. multiple aspects of the SNN
optimised together)
