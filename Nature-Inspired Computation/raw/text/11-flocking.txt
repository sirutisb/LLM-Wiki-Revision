--- page 4 ---
Flocking
Characteristics of flocking
Rapid directed movement of the whole flock
Reactivity to predators (flash expansion, fountain effect)
Reactivity to obstacles and no collision between flock members
Coalescing and splitting of flocks
Tolerant of movement within flocks – loss or gain of flock members
No dedicated leader
Seems to occur in
All media (air, water, land) and many animal families (insects, fish,
birds, mammals. . . ) of different ages and sizes
From small groups (e.g. two geese) to enormous groups (e.g. herring
shoals 17 miles long)
In some animals only in special circumstances (e.g. migration)

--- page 6 ---
Benefits of flocking
Improved accuracy and endurance – Geese flying in V-formation can
extend their range by over 70% and individual geese can fly flaster in
flocks.
Reducing turbulence – fish cast off slime as they swim which reduces
turbulence and (therefore) energy needed to swim.
Repelling predators – Flash expansion, fountain effect in fish;
pronging in antelopes (causes visual confusion in predators); schooling
in fish may confuse predators

Craig Reynolds’s Boids (1987)
A computer animator who wanted to find a way of animating flocks that
would be realistic, 3D & computationally efficient
Local, omni-directional sensory system
Relative range bearing system – detect position &
bearing of all teammates within radius (no occlusion)
Can perfectly identify all the teammates within the
range of detection
Immediate response – one perception-to-action loop
(no sensory, computational capacity considered)
Homogeneous system (all boids have exactly the
same sensory system)
No noise in the range and bearing measurement
Second order variables (velocity) estimated with 2
first order measures (position)

Reynolds’ rules for flocking
1 Separation: steer to avoid crowding local flockmates
2 Alignment/velocity matching: steer towards the average heading of
flockmates
3 Cohesion: steer to move toward the average position of local
flockmates

Characteristics of Reynolds’ flocks
They spontaneously polarise
They synchronise their changes in direction
Flocks join when they meet
If started together flash expansion occurs
If started too far apart they may slowly aggregate – or form
flockettes which later merge (given sufficient time and a small
enough space)

Fault detection
Important to detect and isolate faulty agents in case they infect
other agents
Fault detection methods are often centralized – problematic as it
introduces a single point of failure
Monitor communications between agents and classify according to
expected or unexpected – then examine the agents responsible for
the unexpected communications
Focus is often on homogeneous agents – agents are often
heterogeneous
Approaches are often resource hungry
How should a faulty agent be isolated?
