### Answer

#### Part (a)(i) [2 marks]

**Emergence** refers to the phenomenon whereby complex, intelligent, or adaptive group-level behaviour arises from the interactions of many simple individuals following local rules, even though no individual agent is capable of that behaviour on its own and no central controller directs it. The whole exhibits properties that are more than the sum of its parts.

See [[concepts/swarm-intelligence]].

#### Part (a)(ii) [2 marks]

In PSO, no single particle knows the global optimum. The swarm-level ability to locate good solutions in a continuous search space **emerges** from each particle's simple velocity update rule — combining its own personal best position with the swarm's global best. The collective thus converges on optima that no individual particle could find alone. Without emergence, PSO would merely be many independent hillclimbers.

See [[concepts/pso]], [[concepts/swarm-intelligence]].

#### Part (b)(i) [2 marks]

**Tournament selection** works as follows:

1. Randomly sample $t$ individuals from the population (without replacement).
2. Evaluate the fitness of each of the $t$ individuals.
3. Return the individual with the highest fitness as the selected parent.

This is repeated each time a parent is needed. The parameter $t$ is the **tournament size**.

See [[concepts/selection]].

#### Part (b)(ii) [4 marks]

As the tournament size $t$ is increased, **selection pressure increases**.

**Why:**

- When $t = 1$, one individual is drawn at random — every individual has an equal chance of being selected regardless of fitness (no pressure).
- When $t = 2$, the best of two random individuals wins — fitter individuals are now more likely to be selected.
- As $t$ increases, the tournament almost always includes the fittest individual(s) in the population, so the fittest individual is selected with near-certainty on every draw.
- At $t = \text{population size}$, selection becomes entirely deterministic: the single best individual is always returned.

Therefore increasing $t$ concentrates the selection on the most fit individuals, raising selection pressure, reducing diversity, and risking premature convergence.

#### Part (b)(iii) [4 marks]

Tournament selection is preferred over roulette wheel selection for several reasons:

1. **Handles negative fitness / minimisation:** Roulette wheel requires all fitnesses to be positive and is awkward to adapt to minimisation problems. Tournament selection uses only relative comparisons — it works with any fitness values including negative ones.

2. **Avoids superfit dominance:** With roulette wheel, a single individual with far higher fitness than all others will dominate the selection wheel, causing diversity collapse and premature convergence. Tournament selection is immune to this because only the individuals within the tournament are compared, bounding the effect of any one individual.

3. **Tunable selection pressure via $t$:** The single parameter $t$ gives fine-grained control over exploration–exploitation balance. Roulette wheel offers no analogous control without fitness scaling hacks.

4. **Computationally efficient:** Tournament selection requires no sorting of the population and no computation of cumulative fitness sums. It is $O(t)$ per selection call.

See [[concepts/selection]].

#### Part (c) [6 marks]

**Simple hill-climbing** maintains a single current solution and, at each step, generates a mutant neighbour. If the mutant is better than or equal to the current solution, it replaces it; otherwise it is discarded. The algorithm never accepts a move to a worse solution (no downhill moves).

**Problem:** on multi-modal fitness landscapes (many local optima), hill-climbing gets permanently trapped in the first local optimum it reaches and can never escape, because doing so would require accepting a worse solution first.

**Local search techniques** extend hill-climbing by allowing downhill moves:

- **Monte Carlo search:** accept a worse solution with a small fixed probability $p$ (e.g. 0.1) at each step, regardless of how much worse it is. This occasional random acceptance allows the search to escape local optima.

- **Tabu search:** at each step, evaluate *all* neighbours and move to the best one even if it is worse than the current solution. Recently visited solutions are marked "tabu" to prevent the search from immediately cycling back. This forces the algorithm to explore beyond a local optimum.

**Why better for multi-modal landscapes:**

- By accepting worse moves, both techniques can cross the "valleys" between local optima and reach different peaks.
- They can thus discover higher-quality optima that hill-climbing would never find.
- However, both are still single-solution methods — population-based EAs ([[concepts/evolutionary-algorithms]]) exploit multi-modality even more effectively by searching multiple landscape regions simultaneously.

See [[concepts/fitness-landscapes]].

#### Part (d) [5 marks]

**The truth table** ($y=0$ only when both inputs are 0; $y=1$ otherwise) is the **logical OR function**.

- **Name of this neural network:** A **single-layer perceptron** (McCulloch–Pitts neuron / threshold logic unit). It is a single neuron with two inputs, a weighted sum, and a threshold activation function.

- **Weight settings:** $w_1 = 1$, $w_2 = 1$ (any equal positive weights work; the weighted sum is $w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2$).

- **Activation function format:** A **threshold (step / Heaviside) function** — the output is:
$$y = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 \geq \theta \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$
with threshold $\theta = 1$ (any value in the interval $(0, 1]$ works given weights of 1 each). The activation function fires if the weighted sum meets or exceeds the threshold, implementing OR logic.

See [[concepts/neural-networks]].

#### Part (e) [10 marks]

A textual description of the required diagram (to be drawn in the exam):

**Axes:** x-axis = Model Complexity (low → high); y-axis = Error (low → high).

**Training error trajectory:** Monotonically decreasing as model complexity increases. A simple model (few parameters) cannot fit the training data well (high training error). As complexity increases, the model fits the training data better and better, so training error continuously falls toward zero.

**Generalisation error trajectory:** U-shaped (convex). At low complexity the model is too simple to capture the true pattern — both training and generalisation errors are high (underfitting zone). As complexity increases, the generalisation error initially falls. At the optimal complexity it reaches a minimum. Beyond that point, the model begins to memorise training noise rather than learning the true underlying pattern, and generalisation error rises again even as training error continues to fall (overfitting zone).

**Labelling:**
- **Underfitting region:** to the **left** of the generalisation error minimum — both errors are high; the model is too simple.
- **Overfitting region:** to the **right** of the generalisation error minimum — training error is low but generalisation error is rising; the model is too complex and has memorised the training data.
- The **optimal model complexity** sits at the minimum of the generalisation error curve.

The gap between the training error and generalisation error curves is largest in the overfitting region and narrowest near the optimal point.

See [[concepts/neural-networks]].

#### Part (f) [5 marks]

Conway's Game of Life (1970) uses a 2D grid with binary states (alive/dead) and a **Moore neighbourhood** (8 surrounding cells). The three rules are:

**(i) Death of a live cell:**
A live cell dies if it has **fewer than 2 live neighbours** (underpopulation) or **more than 3 live neighbours** (overpopulation). In both cases, the cell transitions from alive to dead in the next step.

**(ii) Birth (dead cell becomes alive):**
A dead cell with **exactly 3 live neighbours** becomes alive in the next step (reproduction).

**(iii) Cell does not change state:**
- A live cell with **exactly 2 or 3 live neighbours** survives (remains alive) — survival.
- A dead cell with any number of live neighbours **other than 3** remains dead.

See [[concepts/cellular-automata]].

#### Part (a) [15 marks]

**Problem and motivation:**
The paper applies a Genetic Algorithm to optimise the setup and strategy parameters of a Formula One racing car simulation. Real F1 car performance depends on many interdependent parameters (gear ratios, tyre compound choices, aerodynamic settings, pit stop strategy) that interact in complex, non-linear ways. Manual tuning by engineers is limited by time and human expertise. The GA is proposed as an automated optimiser.

**Representation:**
Each chromosome encodes the car's configurable parameters as a fixed-length vector. The encoding covers both continuous parameters (e.g. suspension settings, gear ratios) and discrete choices (e.g. tyre compound selection per stint, pit stop lap numbers). A mixed encoding (real-valued and integer genes) was used.

**Fitness function:**
The fitness of a chromosome is evaluated by running the encoded parameter set through a detailed race simulation and measuring the final race time (or position). The GA minimises total race time. Fitness evaluation is computationally expensive because it requires running the full race simulation for each candidate.

**Algorithm:**
A standard generational GA was used:
1. Initialise a population of random parameter configurations.
2. Evaluate each individual by running the race simulation.
3. Apply selection (tournament or rank-based), crossover, and mutation to generate offspring.
4. Replace the population (elitism used to retain the best individual).
5. Repeat for a fixed number of generations.

**Variation operators:**
- Crossover: uniform or multi-point crossover on the parameter vector.
- Mutation: Gaussian mutation for real-valued genes; random replacement for discrete genes.

**Experimental findings:**
- The GA consistently found car configurations with faster race times than a baseline (engineer-tuned) setup.
- The GA discovered non-intuitive trade-offs — for example, pit stop timing and tyre choice interactions that human engineers had not fully exploited.
- The optimised configurations were validated as genuinely faster, not merely overfitted to the simulation, by showing consistent improvement across multiple simulated race conditions.
- The study demonstrated that EAs can be practically applied to real engineering design problems where fitness evaluation is expensive and the search space is high-dimensional and non-linear.

See [[concepts/genetic-algorithms]], [[concepts/evolutionary-algorithms]], [[concepts/representations]].

#### Part (b)(i) — Genetic Algorithms [5 marks]

| Parameter | Effect on exploration | Effect on exploitation |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Mutation rate** | Higher rate → more random perturbations → more exploration of new regions | Lower rate → preserve good solutions; refine known good areas |
| **Crossover rate** | Higher crossover rate → more recombination of existing material (moderate exploration via combination) | Lower crossover rate → more mutation-only offspring |
| **Tournament size $t$** | Smaller $t$ → weaker selection pressure → more diverse population → more exploration | Larger $t$ → stronger selection pressure → population converges on current best → exploitation |
| **Population size** | Larger population → more diverse coverage of search space → more exploration | Smaller population → fewer individuals, converges faster → exploitation |

Key insight: **mutation rate** directly controls the exploration–exploitation tradeoff. High mutation rate breaks up good solutions (too much exploration); low mutation rate exploits known good regions but risks premature convergence. [[concepts/crossover-mutation]]

#### Part (b)(ii) — Particle Swarm Optimisation [5 marks]

| Parameter | Effect on exploration | Effect on exploitation |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **$c_1$ (cognitive coefficient)** | Higher $c_1$ → particles return strongly to personal best → maintains diversity (each particle stays near its own best region) | Lower $c_1$ → particles ignore personal history → faster social convergence |
| **$c_2$ (social coefficient)** | Lower $c_2$ → particles less attracted to global best → more independent search → exploration | Higher $c_2$ → all particles pulled strongly toward gbest → rapid exploitation of current best |
| **Neighbourhood size** | Smaller neighbourhoods (local best) → slower information propagation → particles explore locally → more exploration | Larger neighbourhood (global best) → fast convergence of whole swarm to a single point → exploitation |
| **Swarm size $N$** | Larger swarm → more diverse initial coverage → more exploration | Smaller swarm → fewer particles converge faster |

Key insight: **$c_1$ vs $c_2$ balance** controls individual vs collective behaviour. High $c_2$ relative to $c_1$ → swarm collapses quickly to gbest (exploitation). The random terms $z_1, z_2 \sim U(0,1)$ introduce stochasticity to prevent premature convergence. [[concepts/pso]]

#### Part (b)(iii) — Ant Colony Optimisation [5 marks]

| Parameter | Effect on exploration | Effect on exploitation |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **$\alpha$ (pheromone weight)** | Lower $\alpha$ → less weight on pheromone → ants are less biased by past trails → more random exploration | Higher $\alpha$ → ants strongly follow pheromone → reinforce established paths → exploitation |
| **$\beta$ (heuristic weight)** | Lower $\beta$ → less weight on heuristic (e.g. distance) → more random city choices → exploration | Higher $\beta$ → greedy heuristic exploitation (always prefer nearest city) |
| **Evaporation rate $\rho$** | Higher $\rho$ → pheromone evaporates faster → old trails are forgotten quickly → allows exploration of new paths | Lower $\rho$ → trails persist longer → ants keep reinforcing known good solutions → exploitation |
| **Number of ants** | More ants → more diverse paths explored simultaneously → exploration | Fewer ants → more focused, faster convergence on fewer paths |

Key insight: **evaporation rate $\rho$** is the primary diversity mechanism. Too low: all ants converge to one tour (exploitation only, risk of premature convergence). Too high: trails reset before useful information accumulates (pure exploration, no convergence). [[concepts/ant-colony-optimization]]

$\mathbf{x}^1$ **Pareto dominates** $\mathbf{x}^2$ (written $\mathbf{x}^1 \prec \mathbf{x}^2$) if and only if **both** of the following hold:

1. $\mathbf{x}^1$ is **at least as good** as $\mathbf{x}^2$ on **every** objective: $f_k(\mathbf{x}^1) \leq f_k(\mathbf{x}^2)$ for all $k = 1, \ldots, m$.
2. $\mathbf{x}^1$ is **strictly better** than $\mathbf{x}^2$ on **at least one** objective: $f_k(\mathbf{x}^1) < f_k(\mathbf{x}^2)$ for some $k$.

(This is for a minimisation problem; for maximisation, reverse the inequalities.)

#### Part (c)(i) [3 marks] — Properties making convergence difficult

1. **Deceptive fitness landscape / misleading local optima:** The Pareto front is surrounded by a basin of attraction toward a different (suboptimal) set of solutions. Algorithms are drawn away from the true front before reaching it.

2. **Disconnected or irregular Pareto front/set:** If the true Pareto front consists of disjoint segments, algorithms that assume a connected front may converge to only one segment and miss others entirely.

3. **Highly non-linear / ill-conditioned objectives:** When objective functions have sharp gradients, plateaus, or many local Pareto fronts, population-based search has difficulty locating the true global Pareto front. High multimodality in the decision space also causes premature convergence.

#### Part (c)(ii) [3 marks] — Properties making uniform distribution difficult

1. **Non-uniform or concave Pareto front shape:** A concave (inward-curved) Pareto front has regions that are inherently difficult to populate with a fixed-size population — solutions in the interior region of the front are often missed.

2. **Disconnected or isolated Pareto front segments:** Algorithms cannot distribute solutions in regions of objective space where the Pareto front has gaps; those gaps result in poor diversity.

3. **Extreme spread / very large range differences between objective extremes:** If one part of the Pareto front is very compressed and another very spread out, standard crowding distance or niching metrics spread solutions unevenly — too many in the dense region, too few in the sparse region.

#### Part (d)(i) [2 marks] — Curse of dimensionality: causes

- **Decision space:** As the number of decision variables increases, the volume of the search space grows exponentially. A fixed-size population covers an ever-smaller fraction of the space — most of the space is never sampled.

- **Objective space:** As the number of objectives $M$ increases, the Pareto front becomes a high-dimensional hyper-surface. The number of solutions needed to represent it adequately grows exponentially with $M$. Furthermore, with many objectives, almost all solutions become mutually non-dominated, so Pareto dominance loses its ability to discriminate between solutions.

See [[concepts/multi-objective-optimization]], [[concepts/nsga-ii]].

#### Part (d)(ii) [4 marks] — Two challenges in the decision space

1. **Exponential search space growth:** The number of possible solutions grows exponentially with the number of decision variables. With limited computational budget (finite population × finite generations), the algorithm can only evaluate an exponentially shrinking fraction of the search space, making it increasingly likely to miss the optimal region entirely.

2. **Variable interaction / high epistasis:** With many decision variables, variables interact in complex ways (high epistasis). The effect of changing one variable depends on the values of many others, making the fitness landscape highly rugged and deceptive. Mutation of a single variable causes unpredictable changes to the solution quality, and crossover may frequently disrupt beneficial co-adapted variable combinations, severely slowing convergence.

#### Part (d)(iii) [4 marks] — Two challenges in the objective space

1. **Dominance resistance / loss of selection pressure:** When the number of objectives is large (typically $\geq 4$), nearly all solutions become mutually non-dominated (no solution is strictly worse than another on all objectives simultaneously). This means that almost the entire population lies in Rank 0 of non-dominated sorting, so the sorting provides no discrimination between solutions. Selection pressure is effectively lost, and the algorithm cannot distinguish better from worse solutions.

2. **Exponential growth in the number of required Pareto-front solutions:** The true Pareto front is a manifold in $M$-dimensional objective space. To provide a reasonable approximation with uniformly spaced solutions, the number of required solutions grows exponentially with $M$. A population of practical size is far too small to represent the Pareto front adequately for $M \geq 4$, resulting in very sparse and unrepresentative approximations.

Assuming $\alpha = 1$, $\beta = 2$, $Q = 1$, $\rho = 0.5$, Heuristic factor $\eta$ is the reciprocal of the distance, initialisation pheromone $\tau_{ij}(t)$ is set to $\frac{1}{12}$.**

- **The path searched by the first ant: $A \to B \to C \to D \to A$.**

- **The path searched by the fourth ant: $A \to B \to D \to C \to A$.**

**Calculate $\tau_{BA}(t+1)$, $\tau_{CD}(t+1)$ and $\tau_{DC}(t+1)$ in the above pheromone matrix.**

### Answer

#### Part (a)(i) [10 marks]

**Goal:** Evolve the connection weights of a single-layer perceptron (one neuron) using an EA.

**Representation:**
Encode the perceptron as a real-valued vector chromosome. For a perceptron with $n$ inputs, the chromosome is $\mathbf{w} = (w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n, \theta)$ where $w_i$ are the input weights and $\theta$ is the threshold (bias). This is a **direct real-valued encoding** of all free parameters. Chromosome length = $n + 1$ (or $n$ if threshold is fixed).

**Fitness function:**
Evaluate the perceptron on a labelled training dataset of $(input, target)$ pairs. The fitness is the **negative mean absolute error** (or negative mean squared error) on the training set:
$$\text{fitness} = -\text{MAE} = -\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} |y_i^{predicted} - y_i^{target}|}{N}$$
Higher fitness = lower error = better weights. For classification problems, the fraction of correctly classified examples can also be used as fitness.

**Selection process:**
Use **tournament selection** with tournament size $t = 2$ to $5$. This is efficient, requires no fitness scaling, and provides tunable selection pressure without being sensitive to outlier fitness values.

**Variation operators:**
- **Mutation:** With probability $p_{mut}$ (e.g. 0.1), select one weight at random and add Gaussian noise: $w_j \leftarrow w_j + \delta$, $\delta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. This makes small perturbations to the weight (exploitation) but occasionally makes larger jumps (exploration).
- **Crossover:** With probability $p_{cross}$ (e.g. 0.8), apply uniform crossover between two parent weight vectors to combine good partial weight sets from both parents.

**Parameter settings:**
- Population size: 50–100 individuals
- Mutation rate: 0.05–0.1 per gene
- Crossover rate: 0.7–0.9
- Mutation step size $\sigma$: 0.01–0.1 (tuned or self-adaptive)
- Generations: 100–500 (terminated when fitness improvement plateaus)
- Elitism: retain the best individual across generations

**Experimental design:**
- Split available data into **training set** (for fitness evaluation) and **test set** (for final evaluation).
- Use **cross-validation** to guard against overfitting to the training set.
- Run the EA multiple times with different random seeds to account for stochasticity; report mean and standard deviation of test performance.
- Compare against the delta rule / backpropagation baseline on the same data split.
- Use statistical tests (e.g. Wilcoxon signed-rank test) to determine whether any performance difference is significant.

See [[concepts/neural-networks]], [[concepts/evolutionary-algorithms]].

#### Part (a)(ii) [5 marks]

**Changed representation for an MLP:**

An MLP has multiple layers. For a network with layers of sizes $n_0, n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_L$ (input, hidden(s), output), the total number of weights is:
$$\sum_{l=1}^{L} (n_{l-1} + 1) \times n_l$$
(including bias weights for each layer). All weights are concatenated into a single **longer real-valued vector chromosome**. For example, a 2-input, 3-hidden, 1-output MLP has $(2+1)\times3 + (3+1)\times1 = 9+4 = 13$ weights.

**The encoding change:** the chromosome is structurally identical (real-valued vector) but much **longer** than for a single perceptron. The structure of the MLP must be known in advance (number of layers and neurons per layer), and the EA optimises only the weights — not the architecture.

**Expected differences in algorithm behaviour:**

1. **Harder search landscape:** MLPs have a much higher-dimensional weight space. The fitness landscape has many more local optima, saddle points, and flat regions — premature convergence is much more likely.

2. **Longer chromosomes → slower crossover:** Uniform crossover on a long chromosome may disrupt co-adapted weight groups in different layers. This can worsen performance; encoding by layer (group crossover) may be preferred.

3. **More generations / larger population needed:** The increased search space requires more computational budget to achieve similar convergence quality.

4. **Epistasis:** Weights in deeper layers interact with weights in shallower layers in complex ways. This means the effect of mutating a single weight depends on many other weights — high epistasis makes the landscape rugged and the EA less effective.

5. **EA vs backpropagation:** For MLPs, backpropagation (gradient descent) is typically far more efficient than an EA because it computes exact gradients. The EA is preferred when the activation function is non-differentiable or when the architecture itself is to be evolved.

#### Part (b)(i) [2 marks]

**City ordering:** A=0, B=1, C=2, D=3.

**Pheromone matrix at initialisation** ($\tau_{ij} = 1/12$ for all $i \neq j$, 0 on diagonal):

$$T(0) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \frac{1}{12} & \frac{1}{12} & \frac{1}{12} \\ \frac{1}{12} & 0 & \frac{1}{12} & \frac{1}{12} \\ \frac{1}{12} & \frac{1}{12} & 0 & \frac{1}{12} \\ \frac{1}{12} & \frac{1}{12} & \frac{1}{12} & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Heuristic factor matrix** ($\eta_{ij} = 1/d_{ij}$ for $i \neq j$, 0 on diagonal):

From DIST (rows/columns = A, B, C, D):

$$H = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ \frac{2}{3} & \frac{1}{5} & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

#### Part (b)(ii) [6 marks]

From city A, the unvisited cities are $\{B, C, D\}$. Parameters: $\alpha=1$, $\beta=2$, $\tau_{ij}=1/12$ for all edges.

Numerators ($\tau_{Aj}^\alpha \cdot \eta_{Aj}^\beta$):

$$\text{Numerator}_{AB} = \left(\frac{1}{12}\right)^1 \times (1)^2 = \frac{1}{12}$$

$$\text{Numerator}_{AC} = \left(\frac{1}{12}\right)^1 \times (2)^2 = \frac{4}{12} = \frac{1}{3}$$

$$\text{Numerator}_{AD} = \left(\frac{1}{12}\right)^1 \times (1)^2 = \frac{1}{12}$$

Sum of numerators:
$$\frac{1}{12} + \frac{4}{12} + \frac{1}{12} = \frac{6}{12} = \frac{1}{2}$$

Probabilities:

$$\boxed{P_{AB} = \frac{1/12}{1/2} = \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.167}$$

$$\boxed{P_{AC} = \frac{4/12}{1/2} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.667}$$

$$\boxed{P_{AD} = \frac{1/12}{1/2} = \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.167}$$

**Intuition:** City C is most likely to be visited from A because the distance $d_{AC} = 0.5$ is the shortest, so $\eta_{AC} = 2$ is the largest heuristic value, and with $\beta=2$ it dominates.

#### Part (b)(iii) [7 marks]

**Step 1: Compute tour lengths.**

- **Ant 1:** $A \to B \to C \to D \to A$: $d_{AB}+d_{BC}+d_{CD}+d_{DA} = 1+1+1+1 = 4$. So $\Delta^1 = Q/L^1 = 1/4$.
- **Ant 2:** $A \to C \to D \to B \to A$: $d_{AC}+d_{CD}+d_{DB}+d_{BA} = 0.5+1+1+1 = 3.5$. So $\Delta^2 = 1/3.5 = 2/7$.
- **Ant 3:** $A \to D \to C \to B \to A$: $d_{AD}+d_{DC}+d_{CB}+d_{BA} = 1+1+5+1 = 8$. So $\Delta^3 = 1/8$.
- **Ant 4:** $A \to B \to D \to C \to A$: $d_{AB}+d_{BD}+d_{DC}+d_{CA} = 1+1+1+1.5 = 4.5$. So $\Delta^4 = 1/4.5 = 2/9$.

**Step 2: Apply update rule** $\tau_{ij}(t+1) = (1-\rho)\,\tau_{ij}(t) + \sum_k \Delta_{ij}^k$.

Evaporation contribution (same for all edges): $(1-0.5) \times \frac{1}{12} = \frac{1}{24}$.

---

**$\tau_{BA}(t+1)$** — ants that used edge $B \to A$:
- Ant 2 ($A \to C \to D \to \mathbf{B \to A}$): $\Delta^2 = 2/7$
- Ant 3 ($A \to D \to C \to \mathbf{B \to A}$): $\Delta^3 = 1/8$

$$\tau_{BA}(t+1) = \frac{1}{24} + \frac{2}{7} + \frac{1}{8}$$

Common denominator 168:
$$= \frac{7}{168} + \frac{48}{168} + \frac{21}{168} = \frac{76}{168} = \frac{19}{42} \approx 0.4524$$

---

**$\tau_{CD}(t+1)$** — ants that used edge $C \to D$:
- Ant 1 ($A \to B \to \mathbf{C \to D} \to A$): $\Delta^1 = 1/4$

$$\tau_{CD}(t+1) = \frac{1}{24} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{24} + \frac{6}{24} = \frac{7}{24} \approx 0.2917$$

---

**$\tau_{DC}(t+1)$** — ants that used edge $D \to C$:
- Ant 3 ($A \to D \to \mathbf{C} \to B \to A$): wait — Ant 3 is $A \to D \to C \to B \to A$, edge is $D \to C$: $\Delta^3 = 1/8$
- Ant 4 ($A \to B \to D \to \mathbf{C} \to A$): Ant 4 is $A \to B \to D \to C \to A$, edge is $D \to C$: $\Delta^4 = 2/9$

$$\tau_{DC}(t+1) = \frac{1}{24} + \frac{1}{8} + \frac{2}{9}$$

Common denominator 72:
$$= \frac{3}{72} + \frac{9}{72} + \frac{16}{72} = \frac{28}{72} = \frac{7}{18} \approx 0.3889$$

**Verification:** The calculated values $\tau_{CD} \approx 0.2917$ and $\tau_{DC} \approx 0.3889$ match exactly the given entries in the updated pheromone matrix, confirming the method.

$$\boxed{\tau_{BA}(t+1) = \frac{19}{42} \approx 0.4524, \quad \tau_{CD}(t+1) = \frac{7}{24} \approx 0.2917, \quad \tau_{DC}(t+1) = \frac{7}{18} \approx 0.3889}$$
