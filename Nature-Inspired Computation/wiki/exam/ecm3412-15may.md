### Answer

#### Part (a) [8 marks] — EA Pseudocode with Parameter Settings

See [[concepts/evolutionary-algorithms]], [[concepts/selection]], [[concepts/crossover-mutation]].

**Pseudocode (steady-state EA with tournament selection):**

```
INPUTS: population size N, tournament size t,
        crossover rate p_c, mutation rate p_m,
        generation budget G

1. Initialise: generate N random candidate solutions, store in population P
2. Evaluate fitness f(s) for each s in P
3. For g = 1 to G:
     a. SELECTION:
        Parent1 = tournament_select(P, t)
        Parent2 = tournament_select(P, t)
     b. CROSSOVER:
        if random() < p_c:
            Child1, Child2 = crossover(Parent1, Parent2)   # e.g. 1-point
        else:
            Child1, Child2 = Parent1, Parent2
     c. MUTATION:
        for each gene in Child1, Child2:
            if random() < p_m: mutate that gene             # e.g. bit-flip
     d. EVALUATION:
        evaluate f(Child1), f(Child2)
     e. REPLACEMENT:
        replace the two weakest members of P with Child1, Child2
4. Return best individual in P
```

**Feasible parameter settings:**

| Parameter | Symbol | Typical value | Justification |
|-----------|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Population size | $N$ | 50–200 | Enough diversity without excessive evaluations |
| Tournament size | $t$ | 2–5 | $t=2$ mild pressure; $t=5$ moderate exploitation |
| Crossover rate | $p_c$ | 0.6–0.95 | High: most offspring recombined; 0.7 is a common default |
| Mutation rate | $p_m$ | $1/L$ where $L$ = chromosome length (≈ 0.01–0.05) | One expected flip per chromosome |
| Generations | $G$ | 100–10 000 | Problem-specific evaluation budget |
| Elitism | $k=1$ | Optional | Always retain best (in generational variant) |

For a generational EA replace step 3e with: generate $N$ children, optionally copy the best $k$ unchanged (elitism), and let those replace the entire population.

#### Part (b)(i) [3 marks] — Fitness Landscape

See [[concepts/fitness-landscapes]].

A **fitness landscape** is a geometric metaphor that maps the space of candidate solutions onto a surface where the height at each point is the fitness $f(s)$ of that solution. Two solutions are "adjacent" if one can be obtained from the other by a single application of the chosen mutation operator — the operator therefore defines the **neighbourhood**, and thus the topology of the landscape. Formally: given search space $S$, fitness function $f$, and mutation $M$, the landscape consists of $S$ together with the neighbourhood graph induced by $M$.

#### Part (b)(ii) [3 marks] — Difficult Landscape Feature

A difficult feature is a **local optimum** in a **multi-modal** landscape — a peak that is higher than all its immediate neighbours but lower than the global optimum, separated from it by a "valley" of low-fitness solutions. Any local search method that climbs gradients and refuses worse moves will halt at this peak and never reach the global one.

Other equally valid examples:
- **Plateau:** a large flat region of equal fitness — there is no gradient to follow, so the search wanders aimlessly.
- **Deceptive landscape:** the local gradients point *away* from the global optimum, actively misleading the search.
- **Needle-in-a-haystack:** the global optimum is a single isolated point surrounded by uniformly poor solutions.

#### Part (b)(iii) [3 marks] — Why Hillclimbing Fails

A hillclimber maintains a single solution, mutates it to produce a neighbour, and **only accepts the neighbour if it is at least as good** as the current solution — it never accepts a downhill move. On a complex (multimodal) landscape this fails for three reasons:

1. **Trapping in local optima.** Once the hillclimber reaches *any* peak, every neighbour is worse, so it stops — even if vastly better peaks exist elsewhere.
2. **Single-point search.** Only one region of the landscape is explored at any time; promising regions elsewhere are never sampled.
3. **No crossover.** It has no mechanism to combine partial solutions from different good basins, unlike population-based methods.

Methods that mitigate these problems include Monte Carlo / simulated annealing (occasionally accept worse moves), tabu search (move to the best neighbour even if worse, with a memory of recently visited points), and most importantly **population-based** algorithms such as EAs, PSO and ACO.

#### Part (c) [3 marks] — Emergence

See [[concepts/swarm-intelligence]], [[concepts/cellular-automata]].

**Emergence** is the phenomenon whereby complex, intelligent or adaptive collective behaviour arises from the local interactions of many simple individuals, none of which is capable of that behaviour alone and with no central controller orchestrating them. The "whole" exhibits properties that are *not* properties of any individual part: e.g. an ant cannot find the shortest path to food, but a colony reliably does; a single boid cannot flock, but a swarm does. Emergence is the unifying principle behind swarm intelligence, cellular automata (Game of Life) and evolutionary computation.

#### Part (d) [8 marks] — Swarm Intelligence Terms

Each term is worth roughly 2 marks.

**(i) Cognitive component.** See [[concepts/pso]]. In PSO, the velocity update has three terms — inertia, **cognitive** and social. The **cognitive component**
$$c_1 \cdot z_1 \cdot (p_{ij} - x_{ij}(t))$$
pulls the particle toward its own **personal best (pbest)** position. It represents the particle's *individual memory* of where it has previously found good fitness. The parameter $c_1$ ($\approx 2.0$) controls how strongly the particle "trusts" its own experience; $z_1 \sim U(0,1)$ is a per-iteration random draw that prevents premature convergence.

**(ii) gbest.** See [[concepts/pso]]. *gbest* is the **global best** position — the single best position found by **any** particle in the entire swarm so far. It is the target of the **social** component of the velocity update,
$$c_2 \cdot z_2 \cdot (g_j - x_{ij}(t)),$$
and is the mechanism by which information is shared among particles. (In neighbourhood variants of PSO, each particle has a *local* best from a topological neighbourhood rather than a single global one.)

**(iii) Pheromone.** See [[concepts/ant-colony-optimization]]. In ACO, **pheromone** $\tau_{ij}$ is a numerical value associated with each edge of the construction graph that records the *stigmergic memory* of the colony — how often, and how successfully, ants have used that edge in past solutions. Pheromone is increased when ants traverse an edge in a good solution (**deposition**, $\Delta\tau_{ij}^{k} = Q / L^k$) and decays over time on every edge (**evaporation**, $\tau_{ij} \leftarrow (1-\rho)\tau_{ij}$). It biases the **transition rule** $P_{ij} \propto \tau_{ij}^\alpha \, \eta_{ij}^\beta$, so that ants probabilistically prefer edges with high pheromone.

**(iv) Autocatalytic process.** An **autocatalytic** (or *positive feedback*) process is one in which the output of the process amplifies the conditions that produced it — "success breeds success". In ACO it refers to the feedback loop:

> good path → ants reinforce it with pheromone → more ants choose it → more reinforcement → ...

This is what allows the colony to *converge* on the shortest path in the double-bridge experiment, despite individual ants choosing essentially at random. Autocatalysis is balanced against the **negative feedback** of pheromone **evaporation**, which prevents the process from locking permanently onto the first reasonable path discovered.

#### Part (e) [6 marks] — Pareto Front and NSGA-II

See [[concepts/multi-objective-optimization]], [[concepts/nsga-ii]].

**(i) What the Pareto front/shell is.** In multi-objective optimisation we minimise a vector of objectives $\mathbf{y} = (f_1(\mathbf{x}), \ldots, f_M(\mathbf{x}))$. Solution $\mathbf{a}$ **dominates** $\mathbf{b}$ if $\mathbf{a}$ is at least as good as $\mathbf{b}$ on *every* objective and strictly better on at least one. The **Pareto front (or Pareto shell)** is the set of all **non-dominated** solutions — those for which no other solution improves any one objective without worsening another. Geometrically in 2-objective space it is the trade-off curve; in 3 objectives a surface; in $M$ objectives an $(M-1)$-dimensional manifold.

**(ii) Two desirable properties.**

1. **Convergence (closeness to the true Pareto front).** The set of solutions returned should be as close as possible to the *true* (mathematical) Pareto front. If the approximation lies far inside the dominated region, the decision-maker is choosing among demonstrably sub-optimal trade-offs.
2. **Diversity / good spread.** The solutions should be **evenly distributed** along the front and **cover its full extent** from one extreme to the other, so that the decision-maker can see the *entire* range of trade-offs and pick the one matching their preferences.

**(iii) NSGA-II operations that produce these properties.** See [[concepts/nsga-ii]].

| Desirable property | NSGA-II mechanism |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| **Convergence** | **Fast non-dominated sort + elitism**. The combined parent+offspring population $R = P \cup Q$ (size $2N$) is sorted into ranks by repeatedly peeling off the non-dominated set. Rank 0 (the current best approximation of the front) is preferred for the next generation, so the algorithm exerts strong pressure toward the true front. **Elitism** (carrying parents into $R$) guarantees that good solutions are never lost. |
| **Diversity / spread** | **Crowding distance** $d_i$. Within a rank that cannot fit entirely into the next population, NSGA-II prefers the solutions with the **largest crowding distance** — the perimeter of the cuboid formed by their neighbours in objective space. Boundary solutions get $d = \infty$, guaranteeing the extremes are kept. This automatically spaces the surviving solutions evenly along the front without any niche-radius parameter. |

#### Part (f) [6 marks] — Perceptron: Processing and Learning

See [[concepts/neural-networks]].

**(i) Processing incoming signals.**

A perceptron (McCulloch & Pitts, 1943) is a single artificial neuron. **Labelled diagram:**

```
        x_1 ──w_1──┐
                   │
        x_2 ──w_2──┤    ┌────────────┐
                   ├──► │  Σ  →  φ(·) │ ──► y
        ...        │    └────────────┘
                   │       threshold θ
        x_n ──w_n──┘
              (bias: x_0 = 1, w_0 = -θ)
```

The neuron forms the **weighted sum** of its inputs and applies a **threshold (step) activation**:
$$a = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i x_i, \qquad y = \varphi(a) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } a \geq \theta \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Equivalently, with a bias input $x_0 = 1$ and weight $w_0 = -\theta$, $a = \sum_{i=0}^{n} w_i x_i$ and the neuron fires when $a \geq 0$. For example, with $w_1 = w_2 = 1$ and $\theta = 1$ the perceptron implements the **logical OR** function; with $\theta = 2$ it implements **AND**. A single perceptron can only learn *linearly separable* problems — it famously cannot compute XOR.

**(ii) Learning from those signals (perceptron learning rule).**

After observing an input pattern $\mathbf{x}^{(p)}$ with target $t^{(p)}$ and producing output $y^{(p)}$, each weight is updated by the **perceptron learning rule**:
$$w_i^{(t+1)} = w_i^{(t)} + \eta \, (t^{(p)} - y^{(p)}) \, x_i^{(p)}$$

where $\eta$ is the **learning rate** (typically $0 < \eta \leq 1$). Reading off the rule:

- If $t = y$ (correct output): error is zero → no weight change.
- If $t = 1, y = 0$ (output too low): each weight on an active input is **increased**, raising future activation.
- If $t = 0, y = 1$ (output too high): each weight on an active input is **decreased**, lowering future activation.

The patterns in the training set are presented repeatedly (each pass = one **epoch**); the weights converge to values that correctly classify all linearly separable training data. This generalises in the multi-layer case to **backpropagation**:
$$w_{ij}^{(t+1)} = w_{ij}^{(t)} + \eta \, \delta_{pj} \, o_{pj},$$
where $\delta_{pj}$ is an error signal computed by back-propagating from the output layer using the chain rule (see [[concepts/neural-networks]]).

#### Part (a) [15 marks] — AntNet (Di Caro & Dorigo, 1998)

See [[concepts/ant-colony-optimization]], [[sources/lecture08-aco-detail]].

**The problem.** In a packet-switched network (e.g. the Internet) data must be routed from each source node to each destination node through intermediate routers. Each node has only **local** information; traffic loads change over time, links fail, and topologies evolve. Static algorithms such as Bellman–Ford or OSPF assume fixed link costs and therefore adapt poorly to changing congestion. Di Caro and Dorigo's **AntNet** applies ACO principles to give a **distributed, adaptive** routing protocol.

**Data structures at each node $n$.**

- A **routing table** $T_n[d][k]$ — analogous to ACO's pheromone matrix — giving the *probability* of forwarding a packet destined for node $d$ via neighbour $k$. Initially uniform.
- A **local statistics model** $M_n(d)$ — a running mean and variance of the trip times observed from $n$ to each destination $d$. Used to judge whether a freshly measured trip is "good".

**Two types of agent.**

| Agent | Role |
|-------|------|
| **Forward ant $F_{s \to d}$** | Probes the network on behalf of source $s$ heading to destination $d$. Travels using the *current* routing tables, recording the path and per-hop trip times. |
| **Backward ant $B_{d \to s}$** | Created when the forward ant reaches $d$. Returns along the recorded path *in reverse*, this time using a high-priority queue, updating $T_n$ and $M_n$ at each node it traverses. |

**The algorithm (steps repeated continuously at every node).**

1. **Launch.** At regular intervals, each node $n$ launches a forward ant toward a randomly chosen destination $d$ (with destinations sampled according to recent traffic statistics).
2. **Forward traversal.** At each intermediate node, the forward ant picks the next hop **stochastically** in proportion to the routing table probabilities $T_n[d][k]$ — exploiting current knowledge while keeping exploration. It pushes onto its stack the node visited and the time of arrival; cycles (revisits) are detected and pruned.
3. **Backward traversal.** On arrival at $d$, $F_{s\to d}$ dies and creates a backward ant $B_{d \to s}$ carrying the full path and per-hop times. The backward ant travels back along the *reverse* path using a priority queue (so it is not delayed by the very congestion it is trying to measure).
4. **Routing-table update.** At each node $n$ on the return path, the backward ant:
   - Updates the **local model** $M_n(d)$ with the newly measured trip time.
   - Computes a reinforcement signal $r$ based on how good the trip time was compared with the running statistics (better-than-average → larger $r$).
   - **Increases** $T_n[d][k]$ for the neighbour $k$ that was actually used:
     $$T_n[d][k] \leftarrow T_n[d][k] + r \, (1 - T_n[d][k])$$
   - **Decreases** the probabilities of all other neighbours $k' \neq k$ proportionally so that $\sum_{k'} T_n[d][k'] = 1$:
     $$T_n[d][k'] \leftarrow T_n[d][k'] - r \, T_n[d][k']$$
   This is analogous to **pheromone deposition** on the good path, with implicit **evaporation** on all other entries.
5. **Data routing.** Real data packets are routed *deterministically* (or with very low randomness) using the current routing tables: at each node take the next hop with the highest probability for the packet's destination.

**Parallel with classical ACO.**

| Classical ACO | AntNet |
|---------------|--------|
| Pheromone matrix $\tau_{ij}$ | Routing table $T_n[d][k]$ |
| Construction graph | Physical network topology |
| Ants build a complete tour | Forward ants probe a source→destination path |
| Heuristic $\eta_{ij}$ (e.g. $1/d_{ij}$) | Local queue length / link state |
| $\Delta\tau = Q/L^k$ deposition | Reinforcement signal $r$ from trip-time statistics |
| Evaporation $\tau \leftarrow (1-\rho)\tau$ | Implicit normalisation of $T_n[d][\cdot]$ |

**Experimental findings.**

The authors evaluated AntNet on the **NSFNET** (US backbone, 14 nodes / 21 links) and the **NTT** Japanese national network simulators, comparing against:

- **OSPF** (Open Shortest Path First — the standard Internet protocol),
- **SPF** / Bellman–Ford with link-cost updates,
- **Q-routing** (a reinforcement-learning competitor),
- **Q-routing with bidirectional table updates (PQ-R)**.

Key results reported:

- **Lower mean packet delivery time** than all competitors under varied load patterns (uniform, hot-spot, transient burst).
- **Better throughput** especially as network load approached saturation — AntNet kept delivering packets where the static algorithms collapsed.
- **Faster adaptation** to changes in traffic patterns (e.g. shifting hot-spots) than the deterministic protocols.
- **Robustness** to link failures: routing tables decayed/were reinforced through the normal ant traffic with no special re-convergence phase.
- **Load balancing**: because forwarding decisions were probabilistic, traffic was naturally spread over several near-equal-cost paths, alleviating bottlenecks.
- Computational overhead of the control traffic (ants) was modest — a few per-cent of total bandwidth.

The paper is significant as one of the first compelling demonstrations that a **decentralised, stigmergy-based** algorithm can match or exceed *centralised*, engineered protocols in a real, dynamic, distributed setting.

#### Part (b)(i) [5 marks] — What is a construction graph?

See [[concepts/ant-colony-optimization]].

A **construction graph** $G = (V, E)$ is the *problem representation* that lets an ACO algorithm build candidate solutions step by step. Its components are:

- **Nodes $V$** represent the **solution components** or **decisions** that can be taken when constructing a solution (e.g. cities in TSP, jobs in scheduling, "include feature $i$" in feature selection).
- **Edges $E$** represent the **valid transitions** between successive decisions (which component can follow which).
- **Pheromone $\tau_{ij}$** is stored on each edge (or node) and encodes the colony's *learned* preference for that transition.
- **Heuristic information $\eta_{ij}$** on each edge encodes problem-specific *a priori* desirability of the transition (e.g. $1/d_{ij}$ for TSP).
- An **ant constructs a solution** by walking from a designated start node to an end node, choosing each successive edge with probability
  $$P_{ij}^k = \frac{\tau_{ij}^\alpha \, \eta_{ij}^\beta}{\sum_{l \in \text{allowed}} \tau_{il}^\alpha \, \eta_{il}^\beta},$$
  subject to the **tour memory** that prevents invalid revisits.

The construction graph is what makes ACO **general-purpose**: any problem whose solutions can be assembled by a sequence of discrete decisions — TSP, job-shop scheduling, bin packing, vehicle routing, feature selection, network routing — can be solved by ACO once a suitable graph is defined.

#### Part (b)(ii) [5 marks] — Construction Graph: 5 Variables × 3 Choices

A natural construction graph for a problem with five decision variables $v_1, \ldots, v_5$, each taking one of three values $\{a, b, c\}$, is a **layered (multi-stage) directed graph**: one layer per variable, three nodes per layer (one per choice), and an edge from every node in layer $k$ to every node in layer $k+1$.

```
         ┌── v1a ──┐   ┌── v2a ──┐   ┌── v3a ──┐   ┌── v4a ──┐   ┌── v5a ──┐
         │         │   │         │   │         │   │         │   │         │
START ───┼── v1b ──┼───┼── v2b ──┼───┼── v3b ──┼───┼── v4b ──┼───┼── v5b ──┼─── END
         │         │   │         │   │         │   │         │   │         │
         └── v1c ──┘   └── v2c ──┘   └── v3c ──┘   └── v4c ──┘   └── v5c ──┘

         Layer 1       Layer 2       Layer 3       Layer 4       Layer 5
```

**Structure summary.**

- 1 START node, 1 END node, plus $5 \times 3 = 15$ choice nodes.
- Edges: START → each of {v1a, v1b, v1c}; then between every pair of consecutive layers there are $3 \times 3 = 9$ edges (a complete bipartite connection); finally each of {v5a, v5b, v5c} → END.
- Total edges: $3 + 4 \times 9 + 3 = 42$.
- A path from START to END picks **exactly one node per layer**, encoding a complete assignment of all five variables. There are $3^5 = 243$ such paths (the search space size).

**Pheromone $\tau_{ij}$** lives on each edge between consecutive layers. After each iteration ants reinforce the edges on their tour proportionally to solution quality, and evaporation decays all edges, so the colony jointly learns which choice combinations tend to yield good solutions.

Note: the START node ensures the **first layer choices also accumulate pheromone**, otherwise the algorithm could not learn that some "first decisions" are systematically better than others (see [[sources/lecture08-aco-detail]] — the same trick used for scheduling).

#### Part (b)(iii) [5 marks] — Experimental Design

See [[concepts/ant-colony-optimization]].

To test the formulation rigorously I would conduct the following experiments.

**1. Baselines.** Compare ACO against:
- **Random search** with the same evaluation budget — a sanity check.
- **Exhaustive enumeration** of all $3^5 = 243$ solutions to know the true global optimum (only feasible because the search space is so small here — in a larger problem replace with a strong heuristic baseline).
- **Genetic algorithm** with an integer encoding of length 5 over alphabet $\{a,b,c\}$, for like-for-like comparison.

**2. Parameter sweep — vary one parameter at a time.**

| Parameter | Symbol | Values to test | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Number of ants | $m$ | 5, 10, 20, 50 | More ants → broader sampling per iteration but more cost |
| Pheromone weight | $\alpha$ | 0.5, 1, 2, 5 | High $\alpha$ → exploit historic preference, low $\alpha$ → ignore pheromone |
| Heuristic weight | $\beta$ | 0, 1, 2, 5 | $\beta = 0$ → no domain knowledge; high $\beta$ → near-greedy |
| Evaporation rate | $\rho$ | 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.9 | Low $\rho$ → strong memory, risk of early lock-in; high $\rho$ → rapid forgetting |
| Initial pheromone | $\tau_0$ | uniform, small random | Affects initial exploration |
| Iterations / budget | — | 100, 500, 1000 ant tours | Convergence behaviour |
| ACO variant | — | Basic AS, Elitist Rank AS, MMAS | Variant comparison |

**3. Experimental conditions.**

- **Repetition.** Each configuration run **30 independent times** with different random seeds, because ACO is stochastic. Report mean ± standard deviation and best-of-30.
- **Fair evaluation budget.** Hold the **total number of fitness evaluations** constant across configurations (e.g. 1000 tours) so improvements come from the algorithm, not extra compute.
- **Statistical testing.** Apply non-parametric paired tests (e.g. Wilcoxon signed-rank) between configurations to test whether observed differences are significant at $p < 0.05$, with Bonferroni / Holm correction for multiple comparisons.
- **Convergence curves.** Record best-so-far fitness vs. iteration to compare convergence speed, not just final quality.
- **Robustness.** Test on **multiple instances** of the underlying problem (different fitness mappings on the same 5×3 structure) — e.g. a unimodal mapping, a multimodal one, and a deceptive one — to assess generalisation rather than tuning to a single instance.
- **Hold-out validation.** Tune parameters on one set of instances; report final results on a *different*, unseen set, to avoid over-fitting parameter choices.

### Answer

#### Part (a)(i) [5 marks] — Domination

See [[concepts/multi-objective-optimization]].

In multi-objective optimisation we want to minimise (or maximise) a vector of objectives $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = (f_1(\mathbf{x}), \ldots, f_M(\mathbf{x}))$. **Domination** is the partial order on solutions that replaces the simple "smaller fitness wins" of the single-objective case.

For two solutions $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ (assuming minimisation):

$$\mathbf{a} \succ \mathbf{b} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \big(\forall m : f_m(\mathbf{a}) \leq f_m(\mathbf{b})\big) \ \text{ and } \ \big(\exists m : f_m(\mathbf{a}) < f_m(\mathbf{b})\big).$$

In words: **$\mathbf{a}$ dominates $\mathbf{b}$** when $\mathbf{a}$ is *at least as good* as $\mathbf{b}$ on **every** objective *and* **strictly better** on at least one. Three possibilities exist for any pair:

| Relation | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| $\mathbf{a} \succ \mathbf{b}$ | $\mathbf{a}$ dominates $\mathbf{b}$ — $\mathbf{a}$ is unambiguously preferable |
| $\mathbf{b} \succ \mathbf{a}$ | $\mathbf{b}$ dominates $\mathbf{a}$ |
| Neither | *Mutually non-dominating* — each is better on some objective; no preference without further information |

The set of solutions that are not dominated by *any* other solution in the search space is the **Pareto front**.

#### Part (a)(ii) [5 marks] — Ranking by Non-Dominated Sorting

See [[concepts/multi-objective-optimization]], [[concepts/nsga-ii]].

**Non-dominated sorting** assigns each member of a population an integer rank by repeatedly peeling off the current Pareto front:

```
Input: population P
Output: ranks 0, 1, 2, ... assigned to every individual

remaining = P
rank = 0
while remaining is non-empty:
    F = { s ∈ remaining : no t ∈ remaining dominates s }
    assign rank(s) = rank for every s in F
    remaining = remaining \ F
    rank = rank + 1
```

The result partitions the population into **rank 0** (the non-dominated set — current Pareto front approximation), **rank 1** (non-dominated after removing rank 0), **rank 2**, etc. NSGA-II's *fast* non-dominated sort computes this in $O(MN^2)$ where $M$ is the number of objectives and $N$ is the population size.

**Why it is necessary.** In single-objective EAs, *raw fitness* gives a total order, so tournament/roulette/rank selection can directly compare any two individuals. In multi-objective optimisation, however, two mutually non-dominated individuals are **incomparable** on raw objectives, so selection cannot decide between them. Non-dominated sorting fixes this by mapping the partial order of domination onto a **total order of ranks** — every individual gets a single integer that any standard selection operator can use. Rank-0 solutions are preferred; among same-rank solutions a tiebreaker (e.g. crowding distance) is applied to preserve diversity along the front. Without this scheme there is no consistent way to apply selection pressure toward the Pareto front.

#### Part (a)(iii) [5 marks] — Why MO is Preferable to Weighted Single-Objective Runs

See [[concepts/multi-objective-optimization]].

A naive alternative to a multi-objective EA is to scalarise the objectives as a weighted sum
$$F_{\mathbf{w}}(\mathbf{x}) = w_1 f_1(\mathbf{x}) + w_2 f_2(\mathbf{x}) + \cdots + w_M f_M(\mathbf{x})$$
and run a single-objective EA repeatedly for many weight settings $\mathbf{w}$. This is **inferior** to a true multi-objective EA for several reasons.

1. **Cost.** $k$ trade-off points require $k$ independent runs of a (typically expensive) single-objective EA. A multi-objective EA approximates the entire Pareto front in **one run** because its population naturally diversifies along the front.
2. **Non-convex regions are unreachable.** Weighted-sum scalarisation can only find points on the **convex hull** of the Pareto front. Any *concave* portion of the front is missed entirely regardless of how finely you sweep the weights — there is *no* $\mathbf{w}$ for which the optimum of $F_{\mathbf{w}}$ lies in such a region.
3. **Weight choice is unintuitive.** Weights must be chosen *a priori*, but the decision-maker usually does not know how to express their trade-off preferences numerically before *seeing* what trade-offs are achievable. A multi-objective EA presents the trade-off curve first; the decision is made afterwards.
4. **Sensitivity to scaling.** Objectives often have different units/magnitudes (cost in £, time in seconds, …). Different normalisation choices change which weights produce which solutions, so reproducibility suffers. Domination-based approaches are **scale-invariant** along each objective.
5. **Uniform coverage is not guaranteed.** Even with a fine weight sweep, the resulting Pareto-front samples can be heavily clustered. NSGA-II's crowding distance, by contrast, *enforces* an even spread.

#### Part (b)(i) [5 marks] — Exploration vs Exploitation in GAs

See [[concepts/evolutionary-algorithms]], [[concepts/selection]], [[concepts/crossover-mutation]].

| Parameter | Effect on exploration / exploitation |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Mutation rate $p_m$** | Higher $p_m$ → more random perturbation of offspring → **more exploration** (jumps to new regions of the landscape). Too high → essentially random search; good solutions are destroyed. |
| **Crossover rate $p_c$** | Higher $p_c$ → more recombination of existing good parents → **more exploitation** of regions already known to contain good solutions (crossover is recombination, not random jumping). |
| **Crossover operator** | Uniform crossover mixes parents gene-by-gene → **more exploratory** than 1-point. 1-point crossover preserves longer building blocks → **more exploitative**. |
| **Selection pressure** | Larger tournament size $t$, or steeper rank curve, or higher elitism → **more exploitation** (the best individuals dominate the next generation, search converges quickly). Smaller $t$, flatter rank curve → **more exploration** (more individuals contribute genetic material; diversity preserved). |
| **Population size $N$** | Larger $N$ → more diverse gene pool → **more exploration**. Smaller $N$ → faster convergence → **more exploitation**. |

**Behavioural consequences.** Cranking GA parameters toward exploitation (high $t$, high $p_c$, low $p_m$, small $N$) gives **fast convergence** but a high risk of **premature convergence** to a local optimum. Cranking them toward exploration (low $t$, high $p_m$, large $N$) preserves diversity but converges slowly and may waste evaluations. Good GA practice tunes these jointly — often using time-varying schedules (e.g. *self-adaptive* mutation rate that decreases as the run progresses).

#### Part (b)(ii) [5 marks] — Exploration vs Exploitation in PSO

See [[concepts/pso]].

The PSO velocity update has three terms:
$$v_{ij}(t+1) = w \cdot v_{ij}(t) + c_1 z_1 (p_{ij} - x_{ij}(t)) + c_2 z_2 (g_j - x_{ij}(t))$$

| Parameter | Effect on exploration / exploitation |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Inertia weight $w$** | High $w$ → particles preserve previous velocity → wander far → **more exploration**. Low $w$ → momentum dies quickly, particles settle → **more exploitation**. A typical schedule decreases $w$ linearly from ~0.9 to ~0.4 over the run, shifting from exploration to exploitation. |
| **Cognitive coefficient $c_1$** | High $c_1$ → strong pull toward each particle's own pbest → particles search around their own histories independently → **more exploration** (swarm-wide diversity). |
| **Social coefficient $c_2$** | High $c_2$ → strong pull toward gbest → swarm rapidly converges on the current best → **more exploitation** (and risk of premature convergence). |
| **Swarm size** | Larger swarm → more concurrent samples of the search space → **more exploration**. |
| **Neighbourhood topology** | Global topology (each particle sees the whole swarm's gbest) → fast information spread → **more exploitation**. Local topology (ring, von Neumann) → information spreads slowly → **more exploration**. |
| **Velocity clamping $v_{\max}$** | Larger $v_{\max}$ → bigger jumps → **more exploration**; smaller $v_{\max}$ → fine local search → **more exploitation**. |

Typical setting $c_1 = c_2 = 2.0$ balances cognitive and social pull; pushing $c_1 \gg c_2$ keeps the swarm diverse and exploratory, while $c_2 \gg c_1$ accelerates convergence at the cost of diversity.

#### Part (b)(iii) [5 marks] — Exploration vs Exploitation in ACO

See [[concepts/ant-colony-optimization]].

The ACO transition rule
$$P_{ij}^k = \frac{\tau_{ij}^\alpha \, \eta_{ij}^\beta}{\sum_{l \in \text{allowed}} \tau_{il}^\alpha \, \eta_{il}^\beta}$$
exposes the relevant parameters.

| Parameter | Effect on exploration / exploitation |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| **Pheromone weight $\alpha$** | High $\alpha$ → ant choices are dominated by accumulated pheromone → strong **exploitation** of historically successful edges, risk of premature lock-in. Low $\alpha$ → pheromone almost ignored → **more exploration**. |
| **Heuristic weight $\beta$** | High $\beta$ → ants follow the static domain heuristic $\eta$ greedily → strong **exploitation of problem knowledge** (near-greedy behaviour). Low $\beta$ → heuristic ignored → **more exploration** of unconventional moves. |
| **Evaporation rate $\rho$** | Low $\rho$ → pheromone persists for many iterations → strong memory of past tours → **exploitation**, but increased risk of stagnation on a sub-optimal trail. High $\rho$ → pheromone evaporates fast → past experience is forgotten → **exploration** is maintained but convergence is slow. |
| **Number of ants $m$** | More ants per iteration → more concurrent samples → **more exploration**. |
| **Variant** | *Basic AS* (all ants deposit) → broad reinforcement → more **exploration**. *Elitist Rank AS* (only top $n$) and *MMAS* (only best ant; pheromone bounded $[\tau_{\min}, \tau_{\max}]$) → more focused reinforcement → more **exploitation**. The bounds in MMAS specifically prevent pure exploitation collapse by guaranteeing every edge keeps a minimum sampling probability. |
| **Initial pheromone $\tau_0$** | Uniformly high → early ants explore broadly; uniformly low → early differences amplify quickly (autocatalysis kicks in early — more exploitation). |

The dominant tunable trade-off is the **$\alpha/\beta$ ratio** combined with **$\rho$**: pushing $\alpha \uparrow$, $\beta \downarrow$, $\rho \downarrow$ exploits past pheromone aggressively (fast convergence, lock-in risk); $\alpha \downarrow$, $\beta \uparrow$, $\rho \uparrow$ produces a near-greedy heuristic search with constant rediscovery.

## Question 4 — Neural Network for Weather Forecasting (30 marks)

*Imagine that you have been provided with a dataset for a local weather forecasting centre. This data consists of measurements for **temperature in degrees Celsius, barometric (atmospheric) pressure in millibars, rainfall in millimetres, wind velocity in kilometres per hour and wind direction (N, S, E, W)** in addition to a record of the predominant weather in the area each day (i.e. **bright and clear, cloudy, showers, heavy rainfall, storms**). The forecasting centre would like you to build a model that will predict the predominant weather for the local area for a given day given the hourly timeseries data for the previous 24 hours. They will provide you with five years of historical data for you to build your model with. You decide to do this using a neural network.*

**(a) Explain how you would represent the input and output data to the network.**

**(8 marks)**

**(b) Describe the neural network architecture that you would use to process the data and explain your decisions.**

**(8 marks)**

**(c) Explain how you would divide up the data and describe the strategy that you would use to prevent the network from overfitting.**

**(8 marks)**

**(d) Describe the runs you would conduct to determine the performance of the neural network on the problem.**

**(6 marks)**

**(Total 30 marks)**

### Answer

#### Part (a) [8 marks] — Input and Output Representation

See [[concepts/neural-networks]].

**Inputs.** Each training example corresponds to *one day* and consists of the previous **24 hours** of hourly readings of five raw measurements. Each measurement must be pre-processed appropriately for a neural network (real-valued numeric inputs scaled to a comparable range).

| Raw variable | Pre-processing | Inputs produced |
|--------------|----------------|----------------|
| Temperature (°C) | Standardise to zero mean and unit variance (or min–max scale into $[0,1]$) — neural nets are sensitive to input scale | 1 numeric input per hour |
| Pressure (mbar) | Same standardisation | 1 input per hour |
| Rainfall (mm) | Same standardisation (or log-transform first, since rainfall is heavy-tailed) | 1 input per hour |
| Wind velocity (km/h) | Same standardisation | 1 input per hour |
| Wind direction (N,S,E,W) | **Categorical** — must NOT be encoded as 1/2/3/4 (would imply a meaningless ordering). Use **one-hot encoding**: 4 binary inputs per hour, exactly one set to 1. (Even better: encode as $(\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$ on the compass to preserve cyclic structure — N is adjacent to E and W.) | 4 inputs per hour (one-hot) |

**Input vector size.** Per hour: $1+1+1+1+4 = 8$ inputs. Over 24 hours: $24 \times 8 = \mathbf{192}$ inputs to the network per example. (Alternatively, summary statistics — mean / min / max / std over the 24-hour window — could shrink this; or a recurrent / convolutional architecture could process the time series natively.)

**Outputs.** The target is a **categorical** prediction over five mutually exclusive classes: *bright and clear, cloudy, showers, heavy rainfall, storms*. Encode as **one-hot vectors** of length 5:

| Class | Encoding |
|-------|----------|
| Bright and clear | (1, 0, 0, 0, 0) |
| Cloudy | (0, 1, 0, 0, 0) |
| Showers | (0, 0, 1, 0, 0) |
| Heavy rainfall | (0, 0, 0, 1, 0) |
| Storms | (0, 0, 0, 0, 1) |

The output layer therefore has **5 neurons** with a **softmax** activation, so outputs sum to 1 and can be interpreted as class probabilities. The predicted class is $\arg\max$ over the five output values.

A single example is therefore the pair (input vector of 192 reals, output one-hot vector of 5 binaries). Five years of daily data gives roughly $5 \times 365 \approx 1825$ examples.

---

#### Part (b) [8 marks] — Network Architecture

See [[concepts/neural-networks]].

I would use a **feed-forward multi-layer perceptron (MLP)** as the baseline, with the following architecture:

```
Input layer (192 neurons)
        │
        ▼
Hidden layer 1 (~64 neurons, ReLU activation)
        │
        ▼
Hidden layer 2 (~32 neurons, ReLU activation)
        │
        ▼
Output layer (5 neurons, softmax activation)
```

**Design decisions and justification.**

1. **MLP over a single perceptron.** The mapping from weather time-series to predominant-weather class is highly non-linear and certainly not linearly separable — a single perceptron cannot represent the XOR function let alone a 192-input weather classifier. Adding hidden layers gives the universal-approximator property (Rumelhardt & McClelland, 1986).
2. **Two hidden layers, modest width.** With only ~1825 daily examples a very deep / wide network would over-parameterise the problem and overfit (≈ 200 inputs vs ≈ 1825 examples is already a strain). Two hidden layers of decreasing width is a reasonable compromise; the exact widths are tuned by validation.
3. **Hidden activation: ReLU (or sigmoid).** ReLU avoids vanishing gradients and trains faster. Sigmoid is the classical choice and is fine here too — but its derivative $f'(a) = f(a)(1-f(a))$ can saturate (cf. [[concepts/neural-networks]]).
4. **Output: 5-way softmax with cross-entropy loss.** The task is multi-class classification, not regression — softmax produces a proper probability distribution and cross-entropy is the canonical loss.
5. **Optional: RNN / 1-D CNN for the time-series.** Because the input is a 24-hour sequence, a **recurrent neural network** (LSTM/GRU) or a **1-D convolutional network** could process the temporal structure natively rather than flattening to 192 inputs — this would likely improve performance, especially with more data.
6. **Optional: separate SOM for clustering / visualisation.** A [[concepts/self-organising-maps|SOM]] can be useful for visualising the structure of the input data and finding clusters (e.g. atmospheric "regimes") to feed in as additional features.

**Capacity rule of thumb.** Total weights $\approx 192 \times 64 + 64 \times 32 + 32 \times 5 \approx 14\,500$. This sits within an order of magnitude of the number of training examples and is therefore a reasonable starting capacity — clearly something we will validate empirically.

---

#### Part (c) [8 marks] — Data Splitting and Overfitting Prevention

See [[concepts/neural-networks]].

**Splitting the data.** Five years of daily data gives about 1825 examples. Because the data are a *time series* (consecutive days are correlated), the split must respect temporal order to avoid information leakage. A reasonable split:

| Split | Period | Size | Use |
|-------|--------|------|-----|
| **Training set** | Years 1–3 | ≈ 1095 days (60 %) | Optimise weights via backpropagation |
| **Validation set** | Year 4 | ≈ 365 days (20 %) | Hyper-parameter tuning, model selection, early-stopping decisions |
| **Test set** | Year 5 | ≈ 365 days (20 %) | **Held-out**; touched only once at the end to estimate generalisation |

(For a more robust estimate, **time-series cross-validation** with a rolling window — train on years 1–k, validate on year k+1, slide forward — averages over multiple folds.)

It is also good practice to **stratify** by class within each split so that all five weather classes are represented, even if the class distribution is skewed (storms are rarer than cloudy days).

**Strategies to prevent overfitting.**

1. **Early stopping.** Monitor validation-set loss after each training epoch. Training loss keeps decreasing monotonically, but validation loss reaches a minimum and then *rises* — that turning point marks the onset of overfitting. Stop training there and use the corresponding weight snapshot. This is the single most effective and standard technique.
2. **L1 / L2 weight regularisation.** Add a penalty $\lambda \sum_{ij} w_{ij}^2$ (L2 / "weight decay") or $\lambda \sum_{ij} |w_{ij}|$ (L1) to the loss. This discourages large weights and hence overly complex decision boundaries.
3. **Dropout.** During training, randomly "drop" a fraction (e.g. 30 %) of the hidden units each minibatch. The remaining units must compensate, producing more robust, less co-adapted features. At test time all units are used with weights scaled accordingly.
4. **Limit model capacity.** Use the smallest hidden layer widths that still fit the training data — fewer parameters, less capacity to memorise noise. Tune via the validation set.
5. **Data augmentation.** Slightly perturb numerical inputs with Gaussian noise during training. This effectively enlarges the training set and improves generalisation.
6. **Ensembling / cross-validation.** Train several networks with different random initialisations on different cross-validation folds and average their predictions; ensembling reduces variance.
7. **Input normalisation.** Already mentioned in part (a) — ensures no single feature dominates the gradient and helps convergence.

The diagram of training error vs validation error (training error monotonically falling, validation error U-shaped) defines the operating point for early stopping (cf. [[exam/ecm3412-19may]] Q1(e)).

---

#### Part (d) [6 marks] — Experimental Runs for Performance Estimation

To produce a defensible estimate of the network's performance I would run the following experiments.

**1. Multiple random initialisations.** Neural-network training is non-deterministic (random initial weights, stochastic mini-batches, dropout). Train the **same architecture** with at least **30 different random seeds** and report mean ± standard deviation of every metric, not a single run.

**2. Hyper-parameter search.** Grid- or random-search over:
- hidden layer widths (e.g. {32, 64, 128} per layer),
- learning rate (e.g. {$10^{-2}, 10^{-3}, 10^{-4}$}),
- L2 regularisation strength ($\lambda \in \{0, 10^{-4}, 10^{-3}\}$),
- dropout rate ($\{0, 0.2, 0.5\}$),
- minibatch size ($\{16, 64, 256\}$).
Use the validation set (not the test set) to choose the best configuration.

**3. Time-series cross-validation.** Use rolling-window CV over the five years to average out year-to-year variability, since one year may be unusually atypical.

**4. Performance metrics.** Because the classes are imbalanced (cloudy ≫ storms), report multiple metrics:
- **Overall classification accuracy** (% correct).
- **Per-class precision, recall, F1**.
- **Confusion matrix** — shows which classes get confused (e.g. *showers* vs *heavy rainfall*).
- **Macro-averaged F1** to weight rare classes equally with common ones.
- **Calibration** of the softmax probabilities (Brier score / reliability diagrams) — does a "80 % storm" prediction actually rain hard 80 % of the time?

**5. Baselines.** To know whether the MLP is doing anything useful, compare against:
- A **majority-class baseline** (always predict "cloudy"), giving the lower bound any classifier should beat.
- A **persistence baseline** (predict tomorrow = today), the simplest weather forecaster.
- A simple **logistic regression** to see whether non-linearity is actually buying anything.
- (Optional) An **RNN / LSTM** that consumes the 24-hour sequence natively — to test whether temporal modelling helps.

**6. Final report.** Only after the experimental design is fixed and all model selection has happened on the **validation** set, evaluate the chosen model **once** on the **test (year 5) set**. The headline number is this single test-set performance with a confidence interval estimated from the 30 random seeds. Discuss generalisation, failure modes (from the confusion matrix), and limitations (e.g. unusual weather in year 5).
