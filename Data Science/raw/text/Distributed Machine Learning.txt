Introduction
• One of the most important aspects to consider when implementing distributed algorithms is 
the patterns of communication. 
• One thing we want to avoid is for one machine to be waiting idle for another machine.

--- page 3 ---
Communication patterns
• Push
Machine A sends data to Machine B

--- page 4 ---
Communication patterns
• Pull
Machine B requests data from Machine A

--- page 5 ---
Communication patterns
• Broadcast
Machine A sends data to many machines

--- page 6 ---
Communication patterns
• Reduce
Compute some reduction (usually a sum) of data on multiple machines C1, C2, …, Cn and materialize 
the result on one machine B

--- page 7 ---
Communication patterns
• All-reduce
Compute some reduction (usually a sum) of data on multiple machines C1, C2, …, Cn and materialize 
the result on all those machines

--- page 8 ---
Communication patterns
• Wait
One machine pauses its computation and waits on a signal from another machine

--- page 9 ---
Communication patterns
• Barrier
Many machines wait until all those machines reach a point in their execution, then continue from there

--- page 10 ---
Overlapping computation and communication
• Communication over the network  can be slow
we want to hide this latency
• An important principle of distributed computing is overlapping computation and 
communication
For the best performance, we want our workers to still be doing useful work while communication is 
going on 
 
instead of stop and wait for the communication to finish

reduce
• All-reduce gives us a simple way of running learning algorithms such as SGD in a distributed 
fashion.
•
Epoch means one pass over the full training set
•
Batch means that you use all your data to compute the gradient during one iteration.
•
Minibatch means you only take a subset of all your data during one iteration.

--- page 12 ---
Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with All-
reduce
• If the are M worker machines such that B = M · B’
• Now, we assign the computation of the sum when m = 1 to worker 1, m = 2 to worker 2, etc. 
• After all the gradients are computed, we can perform the outer sum with an all-reduce operation
• After this all-reduce, the whole sum (which is essentially the minibatch gradient) will be present 
on all the machines 
so each machine can now update its copy of the parameters in the same way
