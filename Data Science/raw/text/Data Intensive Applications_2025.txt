Reliability

--- page 11 ---
Reliability
• The application performs the function that the user expected. 
• It can tolerate the user making mistakes or using the software in unexpected ways. 
• Its performance is good enough for the required use case, under the expected load and data 
volume. 
• The system prevents any unauthorized access and abuse. 
Resilient systems

--- page 12 ---
Fault-tolerance and resilience
• Fault 
When one component of the system works in an unexpected way
 
Hardware
 
Software
• Failure
When the entire system stops providing the service.

--- page 13 ---
Hardware faults
• System failures frequently are related to faulty HDDs, memory modules, power supply units,
• In large data centres, these faults will happen all the time
e.g., a cluster with 10,000 HDDs will  have on average  one dead disk every day
• Traditionally we used hardware measures to prevent system failures
e.g., RAID for HDDs, redundant PSUs, hot-swappable CPUS
• However, with even larger infrastructures, even with all these measures in place, systems will 
fail
Necessity for infrastructures and solutions who are resilient even 
to individual system failures

--- page 14 ---
Software faults
• Harder to anticipate
Hardware faults happen with a probability but most of the time are uncorrelated
• Can be  present on many (if not all) nodes of a system causing widespread system failures
A faulty monitoring software
A process that is overusing resources
Cascading effect

Maintainability

--- page 30 ---
Maintainability
• The maintainability of a system is the overall cost to maintain a system  operational and updated
Operability
•
How easy it is for the operation team to keep it running
Simplicity
•
How easy it is for  new people working on the system to understand it
Evolvability
• How easy it is for engineers to make changes and update to the system (e.g., for new requirements, for 
increased load)

--- page 31 ---
Operability
• Providing visibility into the runtime behaviour and internals of the system, with good 
monitoring 
• Providing good support for automation and integration with standard tools 
• Avoiding dependency on individual machines 
• Exhibiting predictable behaviour, minimising surprises

--- page 32 ---
Simplicity
• Making a system simpler does not necessarily mean reducing its functionality
Often means removing accidental complexity 
if it is not inherent in the problem that the system solves but arises only from the implementation. 
• e.g., inconsistent architecture, obscure dependencies, poor styling or documentation
• Abstraction is frequently a good strategy

--- page 33 ---
Evolvability
• The ease with which you can modify a data system
• closely linked to its simplicity and its abstractions
