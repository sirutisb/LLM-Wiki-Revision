## Answers / Mark Schemes

---

### A1. Generative vs Discriminative [6 marks]

**(a)** [3 marks] A generative classifier learns:
- The class-conditional distribution $p(\mathbf{x} | y = k)$ — how features are distributed within each class.
- The class prior $p(y = k)$ — how common each class is.

Prediction via Bayes' rule:
$$\hat{y} = \arg\max_k \; p(y=k | \mathbf{x}) = \arg\max_k \; p(\mathbf{x} | y=k) \, p(y=k)$$
(The denominator $p(\mathbf{x})$ is constant across classes and can be ignored.)

**(b)** [2 marks] A discriminative model directly learns the conditional distribution $p(y | \mathbf{x})$ (or a decision boundary) without modelling how the features were generated. It does not require estimating $p(\mathbf{x}|y)$ or $p(\mathbf{x})$.

**(c)** [1 mark] Generative: Naive Bayes (or Gaussian Discriminant Analysis). Discriminative: Logistic regression (or SVM, k-NN, Neural Networks).

---

### A2. Logistic Regression and the Link Function [5 marks]

**(a)** [3 marks]

The logit function:
$$\text{logit}(\theta) = \log \frac{\theta}{1-\theta}$$

The **log function** maps a probability $\theta \in (0,1)$ to $(-\infty, 0)$ — it does not cover all real values and, critically, it maps the linear predictor back to a range $(0, \infty)$ rather than the valid probability range $[0, 1]$. 

The logit maps $\theta \in (0,1)$ to all of $(-\infty, +\infty)$, meaning its inverse (the sigmoid) maps the unbounded linear predictor $\eta \in (-\infty, +\infty)$ back to a valid probability in $(0, 1)$.

**(b)** [2 marks]

Starting from $\log \frac{\theta_i}{1-\theta_i} = \eta_i$, exponentiate both sides:
$$\frac{\theta_i}{1 - \theta_i} = e^{\eta_i}$$
$$\theta_i = e^{\eta_i}(1 - \theta_i) = e^{\eta_i} - \theta_i e^{\eta_i}$$
$$\theta_i(1 + e^{\eta_i}) = e^{\eta_i}$$
$$\theta_i = \frac{e^{\eta_i}}{1 + e^{\eta_i}} = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\eta_i}} = \sigma(\eta_i)$$

### A3. Bayesian Linear Regression and Regularisation [5 marks]

**(a)** [3 marks]

- **Prior:** $p(\mathbf{w}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{w}_0, \mathbf{S}_0)$ — a Gaussian over weights encoding prior beliefs.
- **Likelihood:** $p(\mathbf{y} | X, \mathbf{w}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{y} | X\mathbf{w}, \sigma^2 I)$ — same as standard linear regression.
- **Posterior:** $p(\mathbf{w} | \mathbf{y}, X) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{w}_N, \mathbf{S}_N)$ — also Gaussian.

The posterior is Gaussian because the prior and likelihood are both Gaussian in $\mathbf{w}$ (conjugate pair). Multiplying two Gaussians (completing the square in the exponent) yields another Gaussian.

**(b)** [2 marks]

This is equivalent to **ridge regression**. With prior $\mathbf{w} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \tau^2 I)$, maximising the log-posterior gives:
$$\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\min_\mathbf{w} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i)^2 + \underbrace{\frac{\sigma^2}{\tau^2}}_{\lambda} \|\mathbf{w}\|^2 \right]$$

The prior precision $1/\tau^2$ plays the role of the regularisation strength $\lambda$: a tighter (smaller variance) prior means stronger regularisation.

### A4. MLE for Simple Linear Regression [10 marks]

**(a)** [1 mark]
$$p(y_i | x_i, w, \sigma^2) = \mathcal{N}(y_i \,|\, w x_i,\, \sigma^2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(y_i - w x_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

**(b)** [3 marks]

Taking log of the product over i.i.d. observations:
$$\ell(w) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log p(y_i | x_i, w, \sigma^2) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)^2$$

**(c)** [4 marks]

Differentiate with respect to $w$ (the first term is constant in $w$):
$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial w} = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2} \cdot 2 \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)(-x_i) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i(y_i - wx_i)$$

Set to zero:
$$\sum_{i=1}^n x_i(y_i - wx_i) = 0$$
$$\sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i - w\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2 = 0$$
$$\boxed{\hat{w}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2}}$$

**(d)** [2 marks]

Since $\sigma^2 > 0$, maximising $\ell(w)$ is equivalent to minimising $\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)^2$ — the constant $-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)$ and the factor $\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}$ do not affect which $w$ achieves the minimum. The MLE is therefore the least-squares estimator.

### A5. MLE for Noise Variance [5 marks]

**(a)** [1 mark] From Q4(b):
$$\ell(w, \sigma^2) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)^2$$

**(b)** [3 marks]

Let $u = \sigma^2$ for clarity. Then $\log(2\pi u) = \log(2\pi) + \log u$, so:
$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial u} = -\frac{n}{2u} + \frac{1}{2u^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)^2$$

Set to zero:
$$-\frac{n}{2u} + \frac{1}{2u^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)^2 = 0$$

Multiply through by $2u^2$:
$$-nu + \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)^2 = 0$$

$$\boxed{\hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{w}_{\text{MLE}}\, x_i)^2}$$

**(c)** [1 mark] This is the **mean squared error (MSE)** — the average squared residual. It is the sample variance of the residuals evaluated at the MLE weight.

### A6. Multiple Linear Regression — Normal Equations [6 marks]

**(a)** [2 marks]

$$X^\top X = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{pmatrix}$$

**(b)** [1 mark]

$$X^\top \mathbf{y} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ 23 \end{pmatrix}$$

**(c)** [3 marks]

$\det(X^\top X) = 3 \times 14 - 6 \times 6 = 42 - 36 = 6$

$$(X^\top X)^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 14 & -6 \\ -6 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\hat{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{MLE}} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 14 & -6 \\ -6 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 10 \\ 23 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 140 - 138 \\ -60 + 69 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 9 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/3 \\ 3/2 \end{pmatrix}$$

So the intercept is $\hat{w}_0 = 1/3$ and the slope is $\hat{w}_1 = 3/2$. The fitted model is $\hat{y} = \tfrac{1}{3} + \tfrac{3}{2}x$.

### A7. Gaussian Naive Bayes — Classification [8 marks]

**(a)** [5 marks]

**Class 0 score:**

$$\log p(y=0) = \log 0.7 \approx -0.357$$

$$\log p(x^*=80 | y=0) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi \cdot 100) - \frac{(80-50)^2}{2 \times 100}$$
$$= -\frac{1}{2}\log(628.3) - \frac{900}{200} = -\frac{1}{2}(6.443) - 4.5 \approx -3.221 - 4.5 = -7.721$$

Total class 0 score: $-0.357 + (-7.721) = -8.078$

**Class 1 score:**

$$\log p(y=1) = \log 0.3 \approx -1.204$$

$$\log p(x^*=80 | y=1) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi \cdot 900) - \frac{(80-200)^2}{2 \times 900}$$
$$= -\frac{1}{2}\log(5655) - \frac{14400}{1800} = -\frac{1}{2}(8.640) - 8 \approx -4.320 - 8 = -12.320$$

Total class 1 score: $-1.204 + (-12.320) = -13.524$

**(b)** [2 marks]

Since $-8.078 > -13.524$, the model assigns $\hat{y} = 0$ (**ham**). The message with 80 characters is much closer to the ham mean (50) than to the spam mean (200), so ham is the more probable class despite having the higher prior.

**(c)** [1 mark]

Raw probabilities are products of very small numbers that can cause **numerical underflow** (round to zero on a computer). Working in log-space converts products into sums, which avoids underflow and is numerically stable.

### A8. Logistic Regression — Probabilities and Gradient [6 marks]

**(a)** [2 marks]

Linear predictor at $x=3$:
$$\eta = w_0 + w_1 x = -2 + 1 \times 3 = 1$$
$$p(y=1 | x=3) = \sigma(1) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-1}} = \frac{1}{1+0.368} \approx \frac{1}{1.368} \approx 0.731$$

**(b)** [3 marks]

At $x=0$: $\eta = -2 + 0 = -2$, so $p(y=1|x=0) = \sigma(-2) = \frac{1}{1+e^{2}} \approx \frac{1}{8.389} \approx 0.119$.

At $x=2$: $\eta = -2 + 2 = 0$, so $p(y=1|x=2) = \sigma(0) = \frac{1}{1+1} = 0.5$.

**Decision boundary:** $p(y=1|x)=0.5$ when $\sigma(\eta)=0.5$, i.e. when $\eta = 0$:
$$w_0 + w_1 x = 0 \Rightarrow -2 + x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2$$

**(c)** [1 mark]

The log-likelihood of the Bernoulli model is:
$$\ell(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^n \left[ y_i \log\theta_i + (1-y_i)\log(1-\theta_i) \right]$$

This is the negative of the **binary cross-entropy loss** $H = -\ell(\mathbf{w})$. Maximising the log-likelihood is therefore identical to minimising the cross-entropy loss — they differ only by a sign.
